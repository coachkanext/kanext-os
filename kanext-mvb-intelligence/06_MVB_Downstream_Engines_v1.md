# Men's Volleyball Downstream Engines v1

---

## 0. SCOPE

This is the single authoritative document for all downstream intelligence engines in men's volleyball: Development Intelligence, Pro Transition (overseas-only), Coaching Impact Modifier, and Recruiting Intelligence.

These engines consume upstream outputs (Player KR, Team KR, archetypes, system fit). They NEVER modify upstream values.

**Men's-specific context:** There is no US professional men's volleyball league. All professional pathways lead overseas. This fundamentally changes the Pro Transition Engine.

## 1.3 Development Roadmap

### Gap Analysis
Same computation as women's.

### Trainability Ratings by Component

| Component | Trainability | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| SVR (Serve) | High | Serving is highly trainable. Jump serve development is a multi-year process but responsive to coaching. Float serve can be added quickly. |
| DKR (Dig/Defense) | High | Platform technique, positioning, and serve receive trainable. Handling men's jump serves requires specific progressive training. |
| AKR (Attack) | Medium-High | Shot selection, timing trainable. Arm swing velocity has a genetic ceiling but approach mechanics can unlock more power. |
| SKR (Set) | Medium | Setting technique improves with reps. Decision-making and touch are partially innate. |
| BKR (Block) | Medium | Timing and read speed improve. Height/reach are fixed. Men's blocking at 2.43m requires specific footwork training. |
| IQKR (VB IQ) | Medium-Low | Game sense improves with experience. Elite processing speed is partially innate. |

### Development Timeline Projections

| Current KR | Projected KR Gain in 1 Year | Projected KR Gain in 2 Years |
|-----------|---------------------------|----------------------------|
| 90+ | +1 to +3 | +2 to +5 |
| 80-89 | +3 to +6 | +5 to +10 |
| 70-79 | +5 to +9 | +8 to +14 |
| 60-69 | +6 to +12 | +10 to +18 |

**Men's-specific note:** Late physical development is more common in men's volleyball. Players who arrive at college at 6'2" may grow to 6'5" and unlock entirely different positional potential. Physical projection matters more in men's development than women's.

### Skill Development Plans by Position

#### Setter Development Path
1. **Year 1:** Consistency. Zero ball-handling errors. Pass rating 2.15+.
2. **Year 2:** Tempo. Develop first-tempo setting. Run 3+ plays per rotation.
3. **Year 3:** Decision-making. Distribute based on defense, not habit. Dump threat. Out-of-system setting.
4. **Year 4:** Command. Run the offense. Adjust between sets. Manage situations. Jump serve development.

#### Outside Hitter Development Path
1. **Year 1:** Serve receive against jump serves. Get pass rating to 2.15+. Develop platform for men's velocity.
2. **Year 2:** Attack variety. Add line shot, tip, tool. Hit .260+ with shot selection. Develop jump serve.
3. **Year 3:** Transition game. Kill in transition. Back-row attack (pipe). Serve at an aggressive level.
4. **Year 4:** Six-rotation dominance. All phases above-average. Jump serve as a weapon.

#### Middle Blocker Development Path
1. **Year 1:** Block timing at 2.43m net. Read the setter. Get touches on outside sets.
2. **Year 2:** Quick attack efficiency (.350+ on ones and slides). Transition off block.
3. **Year 3:** Blocking range. Move pin to pin. Seal with adjacents. Dominate the net defensively.
4. **Year 4:** Two-way impact. Anchor the defensive front and attack efficiently.

#### Opposite Development Path
1. **Year 1:** Right-side hitting mechanics. Hit .260+ from right side. Block from position 2.
2. **Year 2:** Scoring volume. Become the go-to scorer. Hit against double blocks. Develop jump serve.
3. **Year 3:** Back-row attack from right. Elite jump serve. High-volume scoring.
4. **Year 4:** Complete right side. Score, block, serve at elite levels. Carry the team offensively.

#### Libero Development Path
1. **Year 1:** Serve receive against jump serves. This is everything. Pass rating 2.15+.
2. **Year 2:** Range and pursuit against hard-driven attacks. Emergency technique.
3. **Year 3:** Leadership. Quarterback the defense. Call plays. Communicate.
4. **Year 4:** Floor general. 2.35+ pass rating against jump serves. 4.5+ digs/set. Elevate everything.

# PART 2: PRO TRANSITION ENGINE

## 2.0 Purpose

**There is no US professional men's volleyball league.** All professional pathways for American men's volleyball players lead overseas. This engine projects overseas professional readiness and placement.

The Pro Transition Engine answers:
1. Is this player ready for professional volleyball overseas?
2. Which leagues and countries are the best fit?
3. What salary range should he expect?
4. What cultural/language/logistical factors should he consider?
5. What is his development ceiling at the pro level?

## 2.1 Pro Readiness Assessment

### Minimum KR Thresholds for Overseas Pro

| KR Range | Pro Viability |
|----------|--------------|
| 90+ | Top-tier league starter. SuperLega, PlusLiga, Efeler Ligi. |
| 85-89 | Mid-tier league starter or top-tier reserve. Bundesliga, Ligue A, Superliga Masculina. |
| 80-84 | Lower-tier European league or strong Asian league. Japan V.League, Korean V-League. |
| 75-79 | Entry-level overseas. Lower European leagues, South American leagues outside Brazil. |
| Below 75 | Not recommended for overseas professional. May consider semi-pro or beach volleyball transition. |

### Pro Readiness Component Requirements

For each position, minimum component KR thresholds for overseas viability:

**Setter:**
- SKR 82+ (elite setting is the baseline for overseas)
- IQKR 78+ (must adapt to foreign systems and players quickly)
- SVR 72+ (pro setters must serve at a competent level)

**Outside Hitter:**
- AKR 80+ (must score against pro blocks)
- DKR 75+ (must pass and defend at pro speed)
- SVR 78+ (jump serve is expected - not optional)

**Middle Blocker:**
- BKR 82+ (blocking dominance is the baseline)
- AKR 78+ (must hit efficiently in a faster tempo)

**Opposite:**
- AKR 84+ (this is the scoring position - highest threshold)
- SVR 80+ (opposites are often primary jump servers)
- BKR 75+ (must block the opponent's OH)

**Libero:**
- DKR 85+ (must handle the world's best jump serves)
- IQKR 80+ (must read unfamiliar international systems)

## 2.2 League-by-League Fit Assessment

### Italian SuperLega
**Profile:** Strongest men's club league in the world. Deepest rosters, highest spending. Champions League contenders.
**American player context:** Limited import spots (2 per team typically). Only elite KR 90+ Americans get opportunities. Language barrier (Italian). Season: October-April.
**Minimum KR:** 90+
**Typical American profile:** NCAA champion-caliber or national team member.

### Polish PlusLiga
**Profile:** Top-tier league. Strong volleyball culture. Competitive with Italy.
**American player context:** More import-friendly than Italy. Growing league investment. Language barrier (Polish, but English is common in volleyball contexts). Season: October-April.
**Minimum KR:** 88+

### Turkish Efeler Ligi
**Profile:** Strong men's league. High investment from major Turkish clubs (Halkbank, Fenerbahce, Galatasaray, Ziraat Bankasi).
**American player context:** Good import culture. Competitive salaries. Istanbul is a cosmopolitan city. Season: October-April.
**Minimum KR:** 86+

### Brazilian Superliga Masculina
**Profile:** Powered by Brazil's deep volleyball culture. Brazil produces more professional men's volleyball players than any other country.
**American player context:** Limited import spots. Portuguese language. Strong competition from Brazilian and Argentine players. Season: November-April.
**Minimum KR:** 88+

### French Ligue A
**Profile:** Growing European league. Paris, Tours, and other clubs investing.
**American player context:** Accessible entry point to European volleyball. French language. Season: October-April.
**Minimum KR:** 83+

### German Bundesliga Volleyball
**Profile:** Solid mid-tier European league. Berlin Recycling Volleys are the standard-bearer.
**American player context:** Good infrastructure. English widely spoken. Season: October-April.
**Minimum KR:** 82+

### Japanese V.League Men's
**Profile:** Professional, well-organized. Corporate-sponsored teams. Strong Asian volleyball.
**American player context:** Cultural adjustment significant. Limited import spots (2-3 per team). Good salaries at top clubs. Season: October-March.
**Minimum KR:** 84+

### Korean V-League Men's
**Profile:** Competitive Asian league. Growing investment.
**American player context:** Limited import spots. Korean language barrier. Good compensation at top tier.
**Minimum KR:** 84+

## 2.4 Pro Transition Logistics

### Visa and Work Permit Considerations
- EU passport holders have easier access to European leagues
- Non-EU players need work permits (varies by country)
- Some countries have athlete visa categories
- Agent representation is strongly recommended for international contracts

### Cultural Adjustment Factors
- Language barrier (Italian, Polish, Turkish, Portuguese, French, German, Japanese, Korean)
- Training culture differences (European club models differ from US college)
- Season length and schedule (most overseas seasons: October-April, which aligns with off-season for NCAA spring volleyball)
- Housing typically provided by clubs at top tiers
- Tax implications vary significantly by country

### Beach Volleyball Transition
Some men's indoor players who do not pursue overseas careers transition to beach volleyball. The AVP (Association of Volleyball Professionals) provides a domestic professional option. Beach volleyball uses different skills (2v2 vs 6v6) but many fundamentals transfer. Indoor KR does not directly translate to beach capability.

| Score | Label | Meaning |
|-------|-------|---------|

### Men's-Specific Coaching Metrics
In addition to standard coaching impact metrics:
- **Walk-on development rate:** Does the coach develop walk-ons into contributors? This is critical with only 4.5 scholarships.
- **International player integration:** How well does the coaching staff integrate international players?
- **Jump serve development:** Does the program consistently develop strong jump servers?
- **Overseas placement rate:** Do players go on to play professionally after college?

# PART 4: RECRUITING INTELLIGENCE ENGINE

## 4.0 Purpose

Same purpose as women's but with critical men's-specific context:
- The men's recruiting pipeline is much smaller than women's
- Fewer club programs feed men's volleyball (compared to the massive women's club system)
- Many men start volleyball later (high school, not club at age 12)
- Multi-sport athletes (baseball, basketball) may be late converts
- International recruiting is a major differentiator

## 4.1 Recruiting Data Sources

| Source | Reliability | Notes |
|--------|-----------|-------|
| Club volleyball stats (USAV events) | Medium | National qualifiers are highest level. Men's club is smaller than women's. |
| PrepDig.com rankings | Medium | Men's rankings are less developed than women's. |
| Off the Block rankings | Medium-High | Dedicated men's volleyball coverage. |
| MaxPreps high school stats | Low-Medium | Many states do not have high school men's volleyball. |
| College coach assessments | High | If available from camps and evaluations. |
| Video (highlight reel) | Medium | Self-selected footage. |
| Video (full match film) | High | Rare but strongest data source. |
| International scouting (FIVB events) | High | Youth national team competitions. |

## 4.2 Recruiting Evaluation Process

### Step 1: Establish Physical Profile
- Height, standing reach, approach jump touch
- Projected adult height (men often grow later than women - projections remain viable through age 18-19)
- Position fit based on physical profile
- Multi-sport athletic background (relevant for projecting volleyball ceiling)

### Step 2: Determine Data Tier
Most men's recruiting evaluations are V1 or lower.

### Step 3: Project KR
```
Projected_KR_Range = [Low estimate, High estimate]
Confidence: 30-50% (men's recruiting data is sparser than women's)
```

### Step 4: Positional Projection
Position changes are common in the transition from club/HS to college:
- A 6'0" OH in high school may become a libero or DS in college
- A 6'5" multi-sport athlete may project as an MB or OPP regardless of HS position
- Late growers who arrive at 6'7"+ may shift from OH to OPP or MB
- Basketball converts often have blocking instincts but need volleyball-specific skill development

## 4.5 Late Development and Multi-Sport Athletes
Men's volleyball has a unique recruiting dynamic: many top players are late to the sport. This creates opportunity for programs that can:
- Identify athletic potential in athletes from other sports
- Project volleyball-specific skill development from raw athleticism
- Develop physical tools (vertical, arm swing) in athletes who have not played organized volleyball

A 6'8" basketball player who picks up volleyball as a junior in high school may project as an 85+ KR middle blocker within 2-3 years of focused development. These projections have wide confidence intervals (30-45%) but the upside can be transformative for a program.

## GOVERNANCE

- All downstream engines consume upstream outputs. They NEVER modify Player KR, Team KR, or any upstream value.
- Development projections are estimates with stated confidence ranges, not guarantees.
- Pro salary projections are ranges based on current market data and require periodic updating.
- All professional pathways are overseas. There is no US men's pro league.
- Coaching Impact Score is a rolling metric recomputed annually.
- Walk-on development metrics are a critical addition for men's volleyball coaching evaluation.
- Recruiting evaluation confidence is inherently lower than college evaluation confidence, and even lower for men's than women's due to sparser data.
- Late development and multi-sport projections must include explicit confidence ranges.
