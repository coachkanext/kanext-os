**KaNeXT LLC | Confidential**

## The Core Question

Can a for-profit operating company serve as the institutional partner of a nonprofit university, controlling operations while the university retains academic authority?

The answer is yes. The structure has been reviewed and approved by the IRS, state regulators, institutional accreditors, and (after extended litigation) the U.S. Department of Education. The precedent is Grand Canyon University.

---

## The GCU Precedent

Grand Canyon University was founded as a nonprofit Christian institution in 1949. By 2004, it was nearly bankrupt with $20M in debt and fewer than 1,000 students. Private investors acquired the school and it operated as a for-profit entity under Grand Canyon Education, Inc. (GCE), a publicly traded corporation, for the next 14 years.

In 2018, GCU restructured. The university split from GCE and reverted to nonprofit status. GCE sold GCU's assets to a nonprofit entity (initially called Gazelle University) for $853 million. GCU then entered into a Master Services Agreement (MSA) with GCE under which the university pays GCE 60% of its adjusted gross revenue in exchange for marketing, enrollment, student support, accounting, and other operational services. The MSA spans 15 years with automatic five-year renewals.

Under this structure, GCE (the for-profit company) provides operational services. GCU (the nonprofit university) retains academic authority, accreditation, and institutional governance. Brian Mueller serves as both CEO of GCE and President of GCU.

**What was approved:**

The IRS granted GCU 501(c)(3) tax-exempt status. The State of Arizona approved GCU's nonprofit designation. The Higher Learning Commission (GCU's regional accreditor) approved the change in ownership and the services agreement structure. In November 2024, the Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals ruled unanimously that the Education Department had applied the wrong legal standard in denying GCU's nonprofit status for Title IV purposes, overturning a lower court ruling. In December 2025, the Education Department formally recognized GCU as a nonprofit institution.

---

## The DOE Dispute and What It Teaches

The DOE initially denied GCU's request to be treated as a nonprofit for Title IV purposes in 2019, citing the 60% revenue share to GCE and Mueller's dual role as CEO and President. The DOE argued that the primary purpose of the 2018 transaction was to "drive shareholder value" for GCE.

This dispute lasted from 2019 to 2025. During the entire period, GCU continued to operate, enroll students, and access federal financial aid. The dispute was about classification for Title IV regulatory purposes, not about whether the operating structure itself was legal.

**What KaNeXT learns from the DOE dispute:**

The revenue share percentage matters. GCE's 60% share was the DOE's primary objection. The KaNeXT IOA is modeled substantially more favorably to the partner institution than the GCU-GCE historical arrangement, both in the headline tuition split and in the direct philanthropic contributions and operational partnership fees that flow from KaNeXT to the partner institution every year.

Dual leadership roles invite scrutiny. Mueller serving as both GCE CEO and GCU President was cited as a conflict. The KaNeXT IOA provides for the founder to serve in an operational capacity (KaNeXT-side leadership and, during Year 1 athletics, potentially a coaching role) while the partner institution retains its own President with independent academic authority. The roles are structurally separated rather than overlapping at the executive level.

Independent governance matters. The KaNeXT IOA includes mandatory conflict-of-interest governance: KaNeXT-affiliated board members recuse from IOA-related votes, an independent oversight committee of trustees with no financial ties to KaNeXT monitors compliance, an independent fairness opinion is obtained at execution and reviewed periodically, and an independent annual audit reviews the financial relationship.

## How KaNeXT's Structure Differs

The KaNeXT / partner institution structure follows the same general framework as GCU/GCE (for-profit service provider operating alongside a nonprofit university) but differs in several ways that address the specific issues the DOE raised with GCU.

**More favorable university revenue share.** The partner institution's tuition allocation under the Full IOA is structured more favorably to the university than the GCU-GCE historical precedent. Direct payments from KaNeXT to the partner institution (Operational Partnership Fee and Philanthropic Contribution) continue annually on top of the tuition allocation, scaling modestly with enrollment growth. The specific split figures are documented in the Institutional Revenue Model.

**Direct campus ownership.** KaNeXT is positioned to acquire the Miami Lakes campus land directly as a Year 2+ capital event. GCE did not own GCU's campus. Under the $1/year ground lease structure described in the Institutional Revenue Model, KaNeXT owns the Miami Lakes land and improvements while the partner institution operates academically on the campus at no real estate cost. This gives KaNeXT a hard-asset basis for its investment rather than relying purely on a services agreement revenue stream, and gives the partner institution a flagship facility without carrying the capital burden.

**Structural role separation.** The founder operates in KaNeXT leadership and athletic capacity. The partner institution's President is an independent role with separate authority over academic governance, accreditation, and institutional administration. The two roles do not overlap at the executive level.

**Integrated operating system.** GCE provides services (marketing, enrollment, accounting) through traditional outsourcing. KaNeXT deploys an integrated institutional operating system that is the operational infrastructure itself, not a service layered on top of existing university systems.

**Athletic and intelligence system.** GCU/GCE had no equivalent of the athletic engine, the intelligence system, the media platform, or the mandate distribution model. These are structural assets that create institutional value beyond services revenue.

**Athletics-Only Year 1.** The KaNeXT structure begins with an Athletics-Only Agreement (AOA) in Year 1, which does not constitute a substantive change under ABHE policy. KaNeXT operates athletic programs autonomously in Year 1 while the partner institution retains full academic authority and institutional governance. The Full IOA activates in Year 2 through the ABHE substantive change process and the DOE change-in-control determination. This phasing is structurally more conservative than GCU/GCE, which did not have a comparable Year 1 athletics-only intermediate step.

## Regulatory Framework

The KaNeXT / partner institution structure is designed for full transparency with all applicable regulatory bodies:

**ABHE (accreditor).** SFBC's ABHE accreditation is in good standing with the next reaffirmation cycle in 2028. The Athletics-Only Agreement in Year 1 does not constitute a substantive change under ABHE policy. The Full IOA activation in Year 2 is processed through ABHE's substantive change review. The Miami Lakes additional location activation is a separate ABHE substantive change submitted in advance of Year 3 operational launch. The structure preserves ABHE's requirements: annual affirmation of the Tenets of Faith by the CEO, institutional mission alignment with biblical higher education, external governing board composition, nonprofit status, and ongoing compliance with Conditions of Eligibility and Standards for Accreditation.

**IRS.** The partner institution is a 501(c)(3) tax-exempt institution. Philanthropic contributions (including future campus-related transfers) are structured as tax-deductible donations to the University, not to the fund. The operating relationship must satisfy the operational test requiring that the nonprofit is primarily operated for tax-exempt educational purposes. GCU maintained IRS tax-exempt status throughout the DOE dispute, validating that operator-partner structures do not inherently compromise nonprofit status.

**Department of Education.** The partner institution participates in Title IV federal financial aid programs. Year 1 operates under existing institutional control with no change-in-control trigger. Year 2 Full IOA activation triggers DOE CIO review. The GCU precedent, as resolved through the Ninth Circuit ruling and the December 2025 DOE recognition, establishes that a for-profit service provider structure can satisfy DOE requirements when properly structured. The regulatory implementation details are documented in the Regulatory and Accreditation Implementation memo.

**NAIA.** The partner institution is not currently an NAIA member. KaNeXT and the partner institution submit an NAIA membership application by the October 15, 2026 deadline for April 2027 Council of Presidents decision and Fall 2027 active NAIA Varsity membership. Year 1 Varsity competitive affiliation is selected from available options (USCAA, NCCAA, independent scheduling, or combination) pending NAIA activation.

**State of Florida.** The partner institution operates under Florida law. The IOA must comply with Florida nonprofit governance requirements, Florida Commission for Independent Education oversight where applicable, state authorization for online programs (Florida's SARA participation simplifies multi-state), and Miami Lakes additional location authorization at the state level.

## The Structural Argument

The GCU/GCE precedent proves that the model works and that regulatory approval is achievable. GCU grew from near-bankruptcy to over 125,000 students and $1.9B in campus investment under this structure. The IRS, the regional accreditor, the state, and (ultimately) the DOE all approved it.

The KaNeXT structure is designed to be more conservative than GCU's in the areas that drew the most scrutiny: a more favorable university revenue share, structural role separation, independent governance safeguards, direct campus ownership, and an Athletics-Only Year 1 that defers substantive change review to Year 2 Full IOA activation rather than triggering it at partnership launch. The IOA explicitly requires full disclosure to all regulatory bodies and builds in the fairness review, independent audit, and conflict-of-interest governance that the GCU experience demonstrated are essential.

The model is not theoretical. It has been tested, litigated, and approved at the largest private Christian university in the United States.
