# PRO WOMEN'S TRACK AND FIELD KR LEGEND -- v1

## Purpose
Tier labels for professional women's track and field athletes. Interprets KR in the context of professional competition, economic value, and global standing.

## Women's Professional Track and Field Landscape

### Competition Circuit
Professional women's track and field operates through:
- **World Athletics Diamond League** - Elite invitational series (14 meets + finals). Top prize money in the sport.
- **World Athletics Championships** - Biennial world championship. Gold medal = peak achievement outside Olympics.
- **Olympic Games** - Quadrennial. The singular career-defining event.
- **World Athletics Continental Tour** - Gold, Silver, Bronze tiers below Diamond League. Primary income source for sub-elite professionals.
- **World Athletics Indoor Tour** - Indoor season circuit.
- **National Championships** - Qualifying events for major championships.
- **World Athletics Relays** - Team relay event. Country-level competition.

### Economic Structure
- **World Athletics equal prize money policy** - Men's and women's prize money is equal at all World Athletics events.
- **Diamond League prize money** - Event winners earn equal prize money regardless of gender.
- **Shoe company contracts** - Primary income source for most professional athletes. Nike, adidas, New Balance, Puma, ASICS, HOKA, On, Saucony, Brooks.
- **Appearance fees** - Top athletes earn fees for appearing at meets.
- **Olympic/World Championship bonuses** - National federations and sponsors pay medal bonuses.
- **NIL/endorsement deals** - Growing category for marketable athletes.

### Key Differences from Men's Pro Landscape
- Equal prize money at World Athletics events (achieved 2023+)
- Shoe contracts historically lower than men's but closing gap rapidly
- Pregnancy/motherhood protections now standard in most major contracts (Nike policy change 2019+)
- Smaller total professional pool but growing participation
- Some events have deeper women's professional fields than men's (800m, middle distance)

## PRO WOMEN'S TRACK AND FIELD KR TIERS

### 98-100 - Global Icon / Transcendent
**Role:** Generational talent. World record holder or contender. Defines the event for an era.
**Competition:** Olympic gold medalist. Multiple World Championship golds. Diamond League season champion.
**Economic:** $1M-$5M+ annual income. Major global endorsement deals. Shoe contract $500K-$2M+/year. Appearance fees $50K-$150K+ per meet.
**Historical examples of this tier:** Athletes who hold or seriously threaten world records. Multiple Olympic/World golds in a single event. Household name recognition beyond track and field.

### 95-97 - Global Elite / Major Championship Contender
**Role:** Consistent medal contender at Olympics and Worlds. Diamond League finalist/winner.
**Competition:** Olympic/World Championship medalist. National record holder. Diamond League podium regular.
**Economic:** $300K-$1.5M annual income. Shoe contract $150K-$600K/year. Appearance fees $15K-$50K per meet.
**Transfer context:** College athlete projecting here is a once-in-a-generation program talent. Very few college athletes ever reach this tier professionally.

### 92-94 - Elite Professional / World Championship Qualifier
**Role:** Consistent World Championship qualifier. Diamond League competitor. Continental Tour Gold series winner.
**Competition:** World Championship semifinalist/finalist. Diamond League regular. Top-5 nationally.
**Economic:** $100K-$400K annual income. Shoe contract $50K-$200K/year. Continental Tour prize money significant income source. Appearance fees $5K-$20K.
**Transfer context:** Elite college performer who successfully transitions. Top 1-2% of D1 athletes who turn pro.

### 89-91 - Professional Competitor / National Elite
**Role:** Sustainable professional career. Competes on Continental Tour Gold/Silver. National championship contender.
**Competition:** National team consideration. Continental Tour regular. Regional championship medalist.
**Economic:** $50K-$150K annual income. Shoe contract $20K-$80K/year. Prize money from Continental Tour Silver/Bronze. Some appearance fees.
**Transfer context:** Strong D1 scorer who can sustain a professional career. Approximately top 3-5% of D1 athletes.

### 86-88 - Fringe Professional / Continental Tour
**Role:** Competes professionally but income is supplementary. Continental Tour Bronze/Challenger level. National championship participant.
**Competition:** National championship qualifier but not consistent medalist. Regional competitor. May represent country at lower-tier international events.
**Economic:** $15K-$60K annual income from track. Most athletes at this tier have secondary income (coaching, corporate sponsorship, part-time work). Small shoe deal or unsponsored. Prize money from smaller meets.
**Transfer context:** Very good D1 athlete. Conference champion level. Can compete professionally but likely not as sole career.

### 83-85 - Semi-Professional / Domestic Circuit
**Role:** Competes at domestic and regional level. National championship participant but rarely finalist. May train with a professional group but income is minimal.
**Competition:** Competes at open meets, domestic series. Qualifies for national championships occasionally.
**Economic:** $5K-$20K from track. Requires other income source. May have small sponsorship or club affiliation.
**Transfer context:** Strong college career but production does not translate to sustainable professional income. Common tier for athletes who "turn pro" but transition to coaching or other careers within 2-3 years.

### 80-82 - Post-Collegiate / Club Competitor
**Role:** Continues competing after college but not at professional level. Club team member. Open meet competitor.
**Competition:** Regional and local meets. Masters/open competitions. May still improve through continued training.
**Economic:** No meaningful income from track. Competes for personal achievement, competitive outlet, or community.

### <80 - Below Professional Threshold
**Role:** Cannot sustain competition at any professional level. May compete recreationally.

## PREGNANCY/MOTHERHOOD IN PRO CAREER

### Contract Protections
- World Athletics and most major shoe companies now guarantee contract terms through pregnancy and postpartum recovery
- Athletes cannot be financially penalized for pregnancy (industry standard post-2019)
- Recovery timeline varies by event group (see File 06 Return-from-Pregnancy Development Pathway)

### KR Impact
- Pregnancy suppression is MANDATORY detection in the evaluation pipeline
- Pre-pregnancy KR is the anchor; post-return KR should be evaluated with full suppression framework applied
- Return timeline varies: sprinters/jumpers typically 9-18 months to pre-pregnancy KR; distance runners 6-15 months; throwers 6-12 months
- Some athletes exceed pre-pregnancy KR upon return (documented phenomenon, particularly in distance events)

### Career Arc Adjustment
- Traditional career peak window for women's track: 24-30 for sprints/jumps, 26-34 for distance, 26-35 for throws
- Pregnancy may shift peak window later without reducing ultimate ceiling
- Multi-pregnancy athletes may have 2-3 separate career peak windows
- Historical evidence shows world-class performance is achievable post-motherhood across all event groups

## GOVERNANCE

- All benchmarks women's-specific. Women's implements, hurdle heights, and events only.
- Equal prize money at World Athletics events is current policy and reflected in economic tiers.
- Pro thresholds are approximate and shift with global depth changes.
- Pregnancy/motherhood suppression is mandatory in all pro evaluations.
- v1 benchmarks estimated from World Athletics results lists, Diamond League entry standards, and published contract information.
- Pro KR Legend does not produce KR; it interprets it.
