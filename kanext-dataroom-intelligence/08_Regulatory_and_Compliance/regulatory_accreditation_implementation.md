# Regulatory and Accreditation Implementation Memo

**KaNeXT LLC | Confidential**

---

## Purpose

This document maps the regulatory and accreditation workstreams required to execute the KaNeXT partnership with SFBC. It is a practical implementation guide, not a legal opinion. Each workstream identifies what must happen, in what sequence, with what regulatory body, and on what approximate timeline.

The key strategic insight: Year 1 operates as an Athletics-Only Agreement that does not trigger substantive change review. KaNeXT deploys capital, operates athletic programs, builds infrastructure, and generates visibility from Day 1 without regulatory dependency. Regulatory workstreams activate in preparation for Year 2 Full IOA expansion and NAIA Varsity activation, not as blockers to Year 1 deployment.

---

## Workstream 1: ABHE Accreditation

The Association for Biblical Higher Education (ABHE) is SFBC's institutional accreditor. ABHE is a USDE-recognized national specialized institutional accrediting agency, recognized by CHEA as a national, faith-related accrediting agency. Accreditation is the regulatory asset that enables Title IV eligibility, institutional legitimacy, and degree-granting authority.

### Current Status

SFBC's ABHE accreditation is in good standing. Probation was lifted in 2024 following institutional improvements. The next reaffirmation cycle is 2028. Accreditation covers undergraduate certificates, associate degrees, bachelor's degrees, graduate certificates, and master's degrees. Doctoral programs are outside ABHE's USDE-recognized scope and are not part of the Year 1 through Year 5 partnership scope.

ABHE requires annual affirmation of the Tenets of Faith by the Chief Executive Officer, institutional mission alignment with biblical higher education, an external governing board of at least five members (excluding all employees except the CEO), nonprofit status, and ongoing compliance with Conditions of Eligibility and Standards for Accreditation.

The KaNeXT partnership structure preserves every one of these requirements. The Athletics-Only Agreement in Year 1 and the Full IOA in Year 2 separate academic authority (SFBC retains) from operational partnership (KaNeXT provides). The institutional mission, Tenets of Faith affirmation, board governance, and nonprofit status all remain SFBC's responsibility and operate independently of the KaNeXT operational role.

### Year 1 Athletics-Only Agreement: No Substantive Change

The Year 1 Athletics-Only Agreement does not constitute a substantive change under ABHE policy. KaNeXT operates athletic programs autonomously. SFBC retains full academic authority, institutional governance, and institutional control. The arrangement is a services partnership structured to remain within ordinary vendor and service boundaries, similar to how institutions contract with athletic management firms, enrollment marketing companies, or technology vendors.

No ABHE substantive change proposal is required for Year 1 deployment. Athletic programs launch, enrollment operations activate, platform infrastructure deploys, and the partnership operates without ABHE pre-approval because no substantive change threshold is crossed.

### Year 2 Full IOA: Substantive Change Process

The Full IOA activation in Year 2 changes the scope of partnership from athletics-only to institutional operating partnership. This constitutes a substantive change requiring ABHE review and approval.

**Process Map:**

1. **Pre-consultation with ABHE Substantive Change Officer.** ABHE policy requires consultation at least 6 months before intended implementation. KaNeXT and SFBC engage the Substantive Change Officer during Year 1 to preview the IOA structure, understand ABHE's expectations, identify potential concerns, and shape the formal submission.

2. **Board approval of Full IOA.** The SFBC Board of Trustees formally approves the Full IOA. Board approval is a prerequisite for the ABHE substantive change submission.

3. **Substantive change proposal submission.** Formal proposal to ABHE detailing the Full IOA structure, governance safeguards, financial projections, academic authority preservation, institutional mission continuity, and compliance with Conditions of Eligibility and Standards for Accreditation. The proposal must demonstrate that the partnership preserves biblical higher education mission, Tenets of Faith affirmation, external board governance, nonprofit status, and academic quality.

4. **ABHE review and decision.** Substantive changes reviewed by the Committee on Progress Reports and Substantive Change (PRSC) require COA approval. Timelines per ABHE policy: 90 days for Substantive Change Officer decisions, up to 180 days for COA-referred decisions. Certain substantive changes may require a focused visit for verification.

### Miami Lakes Additional Location: Separate Substantive Change

The Miami Lakes campus acquisition and operational activation constitutes a separate ABHE substantive change for additional location. This process runs independently from the Full IOA substantive change and is triggered when Miami Lakes becomes operational.

Miami Lakes Phase 1 is projected for Year 3 operational launch. The additional location substantive change proposal is submitted in advance of operational launch with appropriate lead time per ABHE consultation requirements. Main campus relocation, if pursued in a later year, would constitute a separate substantive change at that time.

### Online Program Expansion Thresholds

ABHE substantive change thresholds related to distance education:
- First offering of courses using a new instructional modality up to 49% of a program requires substantive change approval
- Reaching the 50% threshold for distance education (first credential completable 50%+ online, or 50%+ of students enrolled in distance education) requires substantive change approval
- Additional programs, new delivery modalities, or new degree levels within ABHE's USDE-recognized scope require substantive change approval

Online program expansion proceeds through the standard ABHE substantive change process as each threshold is approached. SFBC's existing online programs and recent additions (MBA launched Fall 2025, BSN in development) are existing or in-process expansions. Further program expansion operates within the same framework.

### 2028 Reaffirmation Cycle

SFBC's ABHE reaffirmation is scheduled for 2028, which falls within Year 2 of the partnership. The Full IOA substantive change process and the reaffirmation cycle may overlap. The partnership is designed to strengthen reaffirmation posture by demonstrating financial sustainability, operational capacity, institutional investment in quality, and compliance with Conditions of Eligibility and Standards for Accreditation. Approaching reaffirmation with a funded operating partnership, capital commitment, and demonstrated institutional transformation positions SFBC materially stronger than approaching reaffirmation with existing standalone resources.

### Timeline: ABHE workstream

- Year 1: Pre-consultation with Substantive Change Officer, board engagement, proposal preparation
- Year 2: Formal substantive change submission, review period, decision
- Year 2 onward: Full IOA activation upon approval; ongoing substantive change submissions for additional locations, program expansion, and reaffirmation activities

## Workstream 2: Title IV Federal Financial Aid

SFBC participates in Title IV programs (Pell Grants, federal student loans). Title IV eligibility is essential for institutional viability.

### Year 1: No CIO Trigger

The Athletics-Only Agreement does not constitute a change in operational control or ownership. SFBC retains institutional control. No change-in-control (CIO) review by the Department of Education is triggered. Title IV participation continues without interruption.

### Year 2 Full IOA: CIO Review

Full IOA activation constitutes a material change in how the institution is operated. The DOE evaluates whether the institution remains eligible for Title IV participation under the new structure.

**Process Map:**

1. **Pre-CIO engagement.** Concurrent with the ABHE pre-consultation in Year 1, notify the DOE of the anticipated change. Engage legal counsel experienced in Title IV CIO processes.

2. **CIO application.** Submit the formal change-in-control application to the DOE. The application includes the IOA, governance documentation, financial projections, and evidence of institutional capacity. The DOE evaluates financial responsibility, administrative capability, and compliance history.

3. **Provisional Program Participation Agreement (PPA).** The DOE may issue a provisional PPA during the review period, allowing Title IV participation to continue while the full review is conducted. Provisional PPAs are common in CIO transactions but are not guaranteed. Legal counsel engages DOE early to clarify the provisional participation path.

4. **Full DOE review and determination.** The DOE reviews the structure against Title IV requirements. Key considerations: whether the operating agreement benefits the institution rather than disproportionately benefiting the provider, whether governance safeguards are adequate, whether financial responsibility standards are met, whether the institution's nonprofit status is maintained for Title IV purposes. Outcomes depend on the DOE's assessment of the specific facts.

### Relevant Precedent

The Grand Canyon University Ninth Circuit ruling (November 2024) and DOE recognition developments (December 2025) provide useful context for how for-profit service provider structures have been evaluated under Title IV. The KaNeXT IOA is designed to address DOE concerns that have historically emerged in operator-partner arrangements: governance safeguards include independent oversight, the revenue structure reflects institutional benefit, structural separation of roles avoids dual-CEO issues, and the partnership preserves nonprofit status for Title IV purposes. Each institution's CIO review proceeds on its own specific facts, not solely on precedent.

### Timeline: 6-18 months, largely concurrent with ABHE substantive change process.

## Workstream 3: IRS and Nonprofit Status

SFBC is a 501(c)(3) tax-exempt institution. Maintaining this status is essential for receiving tax-deductible philanthropic contributions, preserving Title IV eligibility, and qualifying for various federal and state programs.

### What Must Be Maintained

The operating relationship must satisfy the IRS operational test: the nonprofit must be primarily operated for tax-exempt educational purposes. The IOA is structured to satisfy this test. SFBC retains full academic governance. Tuition revenue allocation ensures the majority flows to educational purposes. The institutional mission and identity are preserved. No private benefit or inurement occurs.

### Process Map

1. **Legal review of IOA against IRS standards.** Tax counsel reviews the IOA before execution to confirm compliance with 501(c)(3) operational test requirements. The GCU precedent is directly relevant: GCU maintained IRS tax-exempt status throughout the DOE dispute, validating that operator-partner structures do not inherently compromise nonprofit status.

2. **Private benefit and inurement analysis.** Counsel confirms the IOA does not result in private inurement. The independent fairness opinion required by the IOA addresses this directly.

3. **IRS notification if required.** Counsel determines whether a new IRS determination letter is required or whether the existing 501(c)(3) status carries forward. In most cases involving a services agreement rather than a change in corporate form, the existing status carries forward.

### Timeline: 2-4 months for legal review, concurrent with other workstreams.

## Workstream 4: NAIA Membership Application

The lead athletic workstream. SFBC is not currently an NAIA member institution. The NAIA application cycle operates twice yearly: applications by April 15 receive decisions in October; applications by October 15 receive decisions in April. Active membership typically begins the fall following the decision.

### Current Status

SFBC's existing athletic programs (men's basketball and men's soccer, launched Fall 2025) compete in the Gulf-Atlantic Athletic Conference (GAAC), of which SFBC is a charter member. No NAIA membership exists today.

### Application Timeline

- **October 15, 2026:** KaNeXT and SFBC submit NAIA membership application
- **April 2027:** NAIA Council of Presidents decision
- **Fall 2027 (Year 2 of partnership):** Active NAIA membership begins

The 6 Varsity programs planned for NAIA Varsity competition are built Year 1 with infrastructure, coaching, recruiting, and roster development ready for Year 2 NAIA activation. Year 1 competitive affiliation for these 6 Varsity programs is selected from available options: USCAA membership, NCCAA affiliation, independent scheduling, or combination thereof. GAAC continues to serve as a conference home for any additional teams beyond the 6 Varsity core.

### NAIA Application Requirements

- Financial stability demonstrated through institutional audits and capital commitment
- Sports sponsorship meeting NAIA minimum sport count requirements
- Institutional accreditation (SFBC's ABHE accreditation satisfies this)
- Commitment to character-driven athletics per NAIA Champions of Character framework
- Athletics facilities appropriate to sport sponsorship
- Enrollment and retention data supporting competitive participation
- Overall athletics budget adequate to support the sponsored sports

KaNeXT's capital commitment, the institutional investment in athletic facilities and infrastructure, the recruited roster quality, and the coaching staff assembled under the partnership strengthen the application substantially beyond what SFBC could present as a standalone applicant.

### Title IX Compliance

NAIA Varsity program launch requires Title IX compliance across the sport portfolio. Gender equity in participation opportunities, athletic financial aid, and other program support must be demonstrable. The Year 1 buildout is designed with Title IX compliance in mind for the 6 Varsity sports, with gender balance across the sport selection and proportional financial support.

### Timeline: NAIA workstream

- **October 15, 2026:** Application submission
- **October 2026 through April 2027:** NAIA review process, potential site visits, committee interviews
- **April 2027:** NAIA Council of Presidents decision
- **July 1, 2027:** Coaches complete NAIA Champions of Character coaching course within 60 days of active membership
- **Fall 2027:** Active NAIA membership, Varsity competition begins

## Workstream 5: Online Program Expansion

The Full IOA gives KaNeXT authority to develop and scale online programs. New online degree programs require academic governance approval from SFBC and ABHE substantive change approval.

### Process Map

1. **Identify expansion programs.** Working with SFBC academic leadership, identify which existing accredited programs can be offered online and which new programs should be developed.

2. **Faculty and curriculum governance.** Each new online program is approved through SFBC's academic governance process (faculty committee, provost, board as required). KaNeXT provides the delivery infrastructure. SFBC provides the academic approval.

3. **ABHE substantive change submission.** New programs or new delivery modalities require ABHE substantive change approval. Online expansion of existing programs or new degree programs submit through the standard substantive change process.

4. **Title IV program eligibility.** New programs may require separate Title IV eligibility determination. Programs offered entirely online must comply with state authorization requirements in every state where students are enrolled.

### Timeline: 6-12 months per program. Online expansion sequences across Year 1 preparation and Year 2 onward execution.

### Timeline: Concurrent with other workstreams. No separate extended approval process anticipated unless counsel identifies state-specific requirements beyond standard compliance.

## Phased Deployment Strategy

The regulatory timeline does not create an operational delay. KaNeXT deploys operationally from Day 1 through a phased structure that separates Year 1 vendor and services operations (no substantive change triggered) from Year 2 Full IOA activation (requires ABHE and DOE approvals).

### Phase 1: Year 1 Athletics-Only and Services Period

During Year 1, KaNeXT operates through structured institutional agreements that remain within vendor and service boundaries:

**Technology vendor agreement.** KaNeXT OS, KTV, KPay, Dipson, KVision, KStat, KDraw, VoIP, and platform infrastructure deploy as institutional technology services.

**Athletic management agreement (Athletics-Only Agreement).** KaNeXT operates athletic programs autonomously as an external services partner. Coaching staff are hired under this framework with KaNeXT funding. 6 Varsity programs built NAIA-ready.

**Enrollment services partnership.** The enrollment engine operates as a student acquisition and marketing partner. Enrollment services at athletics scope Year 1, institution-wide scope Year 2 upon Full IOA activation.

**Media and infrastructure services.** Camera deployment, KTV streaming setup, KStat integration, and KVision processing deploy as media vendor services.

During Phase 1, KaNeXT funds all deployments as capital investment. The partner institution operates normally with new service providers. Institutional revenue continues under the partner institution's existing structure. The Year 1 athletic season launches. Enrollment marketing activates. Games are filmed and streamed. The OS runs. Intelligence is operational. Infrastructure builds. The institution transforms in practice while the Year 2 governance change processes through ABHE and DOE in parallel.

### Phase 2: Year 2 Full IOA Activation

Upon ABHE substantive change approval and DOE CIO determination, the Full IOA executes. Revenue allocation under IOA terms activates. Governance provisions take effect. Operational authority formally transfers to KaNeXT under IOA terms. NAIA Varsity membership activates concurrently. The 6 Varsity programs transition from Year 1 competitive affiliation to NAIA Varsity.

### Back-Payment Reconciliation

If permitted by counsel and regulatorily supportable, the IOA may include a retroactive reconciliation provision covering the Year 1 vendor and services period. Upon IOA activation, the revenue allocation may be reconciled as if IOA terms had been in effect from Year 1 launch. KaNeXT would be made whole for capital deployed during Year 1 through a pre-agreed commercial term of the IOA activated upon formal approval.

This mechanism requires legal structuring to ensure it does not create the appearance that IOA economics were functionally in place before approval. Counsel must confirm compatibility with ABHE standards, Title IV requirements, and nonprofit governance obligations.

### Why This Works

From ABHE's perspective: the institution adopted new technology vendors, athletic services, and enrollment partners in Year 1 (ordinary services), then in Year 2 formalized a deeper operating partnership reviewed and approved through the substantive change process. The phasing demonstrates institutional responsibility by deploying services before executing operational governance change.

From the DOE's perspective: Year 1 operates under existing institutional control, maintaining Title IV eligibility without CIO trigger. Year 2 CIO review proceeds on a prepared application with fully disclosed institutional transformation context.

From the investor's perspective: KaNeXT is fully operational from Day 1. Revenue economics are preserved through the reconciliation provision. The regulatory timeline does not create a Year 1 revenue gap. It creates a Year 1 services period that reconciles into full IOA economics upon Year 2 activation.

### Red-Line Triggers

The phased deployment strategy is monitored for triggers requiring reassessment:

- If the cumulative scope of vendor agreements begins to resemble governance transfer rather than service provision, Year 1 structure must be narrowed until approvals are obtained
- If ABHE informally signals that the phased approach is being viewed as de facto control change, deployment scope must be narrowed to clearly non-control functions
- If academic oversight boundaries blur (KaNeXT staff directing academic decisions, controlling faculty, directing curriculum), structural separation must be reinforced immediately
- If the SFBC board cannot reach approval on the Full IOA due to governance considerations, the Year 2 timeline shifts and Year 1 scope must be reassessed for standalone viability
- If the DOE frames the Year 1 services arrangement as a control event earlier than Full IOA activation, legal counsel must engage DOE immediately to clarify boundaries and potentially accelerate the CIO process
- If NAIA application is deferred or denied, Year 2 competitive affiliation must be adjusted to preserve program continuity while reapplying or pursuing alternative association pathways

These triggers are decision points requiring real-time legal and strategic judgment. The phased strategy is adaptive.
