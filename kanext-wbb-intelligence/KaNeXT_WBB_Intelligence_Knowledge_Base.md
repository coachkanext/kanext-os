## 1. What is KaNeXT Women's Basketball Intelligence

KaNeXT Women's Basketball Intelligence is a universal player and team evaluation system that produces deterministic, auditable ratings for women's basketball players at every competitive level in the world. It uses the same architectural framework as the men's basketball intelligence system - same pipeline, same evaluation protocol, same governance rules - but with content calibrated specifically for women's basketball.

Women's basketball is its own sport with its own production norms, its own physical benchmarks, its own pro landscape, and its own evaluation context. A system that simply copies men's benchmarks with a discount fails to capture the unique dynamics of the women's game. This system was built from the ground up with women's basketball-specific calibration.

The intelligence system produces a single universal number for each player: the KR (KaNeXT Rating). This number is computed using the same methodology as men's basketball KR but against women's-specific legends, trait bands, and physical profiles. A women's KR of 90 and a men's KR of 90 represent the same percentile of dominance within their respective ecosystems, but they are not interchangeable across genders.

The system was designed by a coach with 10+ years of experience developing players across multiple competitive levels. The architecture has been validated in men's basketball across 152+ players with zero rank inversions. Women's basketball calibration is ongoing and will follow the same validation standard.

## 2. The KR System - Universal 0-100 Rating

KR stands for KaNeXT Rating. It is a single number on a 0-100 scale that represents a women's basketball player's total evaluated ability at the time of evaluation.

### What KR Measures

KR captures the complete women's basketball player: offense, defense, physical tools, and basketball IQ, weighted by position. A point guard's KR weights offense at 54% because creation and scoring are the point guard's primary job. A center's KR weights defense at 42% because rim protection and rebounding are the center's primary contributions, while weighting offense at 36% because post scoring is more valuable in women's basketball than in men's.

KR is not points per game. It is not PER. It is not a box score formula. KR is a composite of 47 individual trait scores, weighted by position, adjusted for system fit, modified by badges and overrides, and checked against system risks.

### KR is Universal

A KR of 85 means the same thing regardless of what level the player competes at. What changes is the interpretation. Each competitive level has its own legend that translates KR values into tier labels. An 85 KR reads differently at different levels:

- At D1 High-Major: Reliable Bench / Rotation Contributor
- At D1 Mid-Major: Starter / Key Rotation
- At D1 Low-Major: High-Impact Starter
- At NCAA D2: High-Impact Starter / Core Winner
- At NAIA: Conference Star

One player. One KR. Multiple legend reads depending on level context. This is the Level Tier Map.

### How KR Stays Universal: KLVN Lambda Normalization

KLVN (pronounced "Calvin") normalizes production inputs before they enter the evaluation pipeline. A player averaging 18 points per game at NAIA is not producing the same basketball output as a player averaging 18 points per game at D1 High-Major. The competition, the athleticism, the defensive quality, and the pace are all different.

Each competitive level has a lambda value between 0 and 1, where D1 High-Major is the reference point at 1.000. Lambda normalizes INPUTS during trait scoring. It does NOT convert KR OUTPUTS. There is no "HM-equivalent KR."

Women's basketball lambdas are similar to but not identical to men's lambdas. The competitive parity within women's D1 basketball is slightly different from men's (for example, the gap between D1 HM and D1 MM is marginally smaller in women's basketball for some metrics).

### What Different KR Ranges Mean (D1 HM Reference)

- 98-100: National Player of the Year. Transcendent. Think A'ja Wilson's South Carolina years, Caitlin Clark's record-breaking season.
- 95-97: Franchise Anchor / Elite All-American. Team's unquestioned alpha. Think Paige Bueckers healthy, Aliyah Boston's NPOY year.
- 92-94: High-Impact Starter / Core Winner. All-Conference caliber. The second-best player on a Final Four team.
- 89-91: Solid Starter. 25+ minutes. Consistent two-way impact. 12+ PPG with efficiency.
- 86-88: Trusted Rotation / High-Minute Role Player. 20+ minutes. Specialty value.
- 83-85: Reliable Bench / Rotation Contributor. 15-20 minutes. 6th-7th player on a ranked team.
- 80-82: Situational Specialist. 10-15 minutes.
- Below 80: Limited bench to non-rotation.

## 3. How Women's Basketball KR Differs from Men's

The architecture is identical. The content differs in these critical ways:

### Production Benchmarks
Women's basketball scoring averages are lower than men's at every level. A 20 PPG scorer in D1 HM women's basketball is more dominant relative to the league than a 20 PPG scorer in D1 HM men's. The women's legends reflect this. At D1 HM women's, the 98-100 tier starts at 22+ PPG; at D1 HM men's, it starts at 28+ PPG.

Assist averages are comparable between men's and women's basketball at the point guard position but lower for non-guards. Steal rates are slightly higher in women's basketball on average. Block rates are slightly lower on average but elite rim protectors exist at comparable rates.

### Physical Tools (TKR)
Women's TKR benchmarks are fundamentally different. A 6'2" women's guard is an elite physical profile (TKR 90+ for height). A 6'2" men's guard is average. A 6'5" women's center is above-average. A 6'5" men's center is undersized.

Vertical leap benchmarks are significantly different. A 28-inch standing vertical is elite for a women's player. Dunking is extremely rare in women's basketball.

Speed and lateral quickness benchmarks are scored within the women's distribution. A women's guard with elite speed is scored 90+ even though her absolute sprint time would be slower than a men's guard with elite speed.

### Post Play Value
Post scoring is more prominent and more valuable in women's basketball than in men's. The C position's offensive OPF weight is 36% in women's (vs 34% in men's) reflecting this. Post Scorer is a primary archetype in many women's systems. Zone defenses are more common in women's basketball in part because post play creates more of the offensive structure.

### Three-Point Line History
NCAA women's basketball moved to the FIBA/NCAA men's distance (22'1.75") in 2021-22, from the previous 20'9" distance. All historical three-point data must account for this change. Shooting percentages dropped league-wide after the line moved back.

The WNBA has used the FIBA distance since 2013. Historical WNBA data from before 2013 used a shorter distance.

### Pro Landscape
The WNBA has 15 teams (2026), expanding to 18 by 2030. The 2026 WNBA Draft is 3 rounds, 36 picks. WNBA roster maximum is 12 players.

Under the new CBA (effective 2026):
- Salary cap: $7M per team (was $1.5M)
- Supermax: $1.4M (was $249K)
- Average salary: ~$583K (was $120K)
- Minimum: $270K-$300K (was $66K-$79K)

Many elite women's players earn additional income playing overseas during the WNBA offseason. The new CBA narrows the WNBA-to-overseas salary gap significantly but does not eliminate it. The Pro Transition Engine projects both WNBA and overseas paths.

### Pregnancy/Motherhood Suppression
Women's basketball has a unique suppression category. Players returning from pregnancy may show suppressed production for 1-2 seasons. The system accounts for this like injury suppression: both the current production estimate and the pre-pregnancy baseline are reported. Multiple WNBA All-Stars have returned to elite production after having children.

## 4. The Evaluation Pipeline

### Phase 3: Production Anchor (The Primary KR Determinant)
The most important step. Read the KR Legend at the player's level. Map the player's production profile against the legend tier descriptions. The tier whose production description matches is the anchor. This is the truth. Everything else adjusts within it.

Phase 3 Anchoring Rules:
- Anchor against PRODUCTION PROFILE NUMBERS, not award labels
- Awards are confirmation, not input
- Pedigree does not inflate current KR
- Team success does not inflate individual KR
- Historical comparisons are irrelevant
- Read the numbers first, check labels second

### Phase 6: Component KRs (Adjusts the Anchor)
Four component KRs scored from the data:
- OKR (Offensive KR): scoring, shooting, creation, passing
- DKR (Defensive KR): steals, blocks, rebounding, defensive activity
- TKR (Tools KR): height, weight, wingspan, athleticism, motor (against women's benchmarks)
- IQKR (IQ KR): AST/TO, shot selection, decision-making

Phase 6 adjusts within Phase 3 +/- 10. The legend anchor is truth. The math is confirmation.

### Position Trait Weighting (OPF)
Women's college OPF:
- PG: 54% offense / 28% defense / 10% tools / 8% IQ
- SG: 52% / 30% / 12% / 6%
- SF: 46% / 34% / 14% / 6%
- PF: 38% / 40% / 18% / 4%
- C: 36% / 42% / 18% / 4%

## 5. Trait Library

47 traits across 8 clusters: Shooting (6 traits), Finishing (6 traits), Playmaking (8 traits), POA Defense (5 traits), Team Defense (6 traits), Rebounding (5 traits), Tools (6 traits), IQ (5 traits).

Every trait is scored on a 0-100 scale using women's-specific scoring bands. The bands are calibrated to women's basketball distributions, not men's. Key differences in women's trait scoring:

Shooting bands are slightly lower than men's. A women's player shooting 40% from three on volume is MORE elite than a men's player at 40% because the league-wide women's three-point percentage is lower.

Finishing bands are approximately 3-5% lower at the rim than men's, reflecting lower average vertical athleticism above the rim.

Rebounding bands are similar in structure but the threshold for elite offensive rebounding is slightly lower (3.5+ ORB/G vs ~4.0 for men's at D1 HM).

Physical Tools (height, wingspan, speed, vertical) use entirely different scales calibrated to women's body measurements and athletic performance.

## 13. Women's Basketball Production Norms

Understanding women's basketball production norms is essential for accurate legend calibration and trait scoring.

### Scoring
D1 HM women's basketball average team scoring: ~72 PPG (vs ~78 PPG for men's). The gap narrows at the WNBA level: ~82 PPG (vs ~112 for NBA, but WNBA plays 40-minute games vs NBA's 48 minutes).

A 20 PPG scorer in D1 HM women's basketball is rarer and more dominant than a 20 PPG scorer in D1 HM men's. Adjusting for pace and team scoring, a women's 20 PPG is roughly equivalent to a men's 24-25 PPG in terms of relative dominance.

### Shooting
League-wide three-point percentage in NCAA women's D1: approximately 31-33% (vs 33-35% for men's). The gap has narrowed since the line moved back in 2021-22 because the change affected both genders' college percentages. A 38%+ three-point shooter in women's D1 is elite.

Free throw percentages are comparable between men's and women's basketball. The best women's FT shooters hit 85-90%, similar to the best men's shooters.

### Rebounding
Women's basketball rebounding averages are comparable to men's at the per-game level because while women's players are generally shorter, the pace is slower (fewer missed shots per minute). A 10+ RPG season for a women's player at D1 HM is elite, similar to men's.

### Assists
Women's point guard assist averages are comparable to men's (5-7 APG for elite PGs). Non-point-guard assist averages are slightly lower in women's basketball because post play creates fewer assist opportunities than drive-and-kick basketball.

### Blocks
Average blocks per game are slightly lower in women's basketball. An elite women's rim protector produces 2.0-3.0 BPG. Blocks above 3.0 are extremely rare and represent transcendent rim protection.

### Steals
Steal rates are slightly higher on average in women's basketball than men's. This is reflected in the trait scoring bands.

## 14. The Three-Point Line Change

The NCAA women's three-point line moved from 20'9" to 22'1.75" (the FIBA/NCAA men's distance) starting in the 2021-22 season. This was a significant change:

Before (20'9"): Higher league-wide 3P% (approximately 33-35% D1 average). More players attempted threes. The line was considered "too close" by many coaches.

After (22'1.75"): Lower league-wide 3P% (approximately 31-33% D1 average). Some players who were effective three-point shooters at the old distance lost range at the new distance. The game initially shifted back toward post play and midrange, but has since adapted with improved perimeter shooting development.

All historical three-point data in the system is tagged with the line distance. Trait scoring for the Shooting cluster adjusts for the line distance. A 36% three-point shooter from 2020-21 (old line) is NOT equivalent to a 36% shooter from 2023-24 (new line) in terms of shooting ability.

The WNBA moved to the FIBA distance in 2013. Historical WNBA data from before 2013 used a shorter distance.

## 17. Transfer Portal Dynamics

The women's basketball transfer portal is extremely active. Key dynamics:

Volume: More players enter the women's basketball transfer portal than men's relative to total roster spots. Portal entries spike after coaching changes, which are frequent in women's basketball.

International factor: Many top international players come to NCAA basketball after high school or after playing in international youth programs. Some international players enter the portal to change schools within the NCAA.

Fifth-year / graduate transfers: Very common in women's basketball. Many players use a fifth year (including COVID eligibility) or graduate transfer to play at a higher level.

NIL impact: NIL has created a secondary market for women's basketball talent. Top portal players at elite programs can command significant NIL deals. NIL is less developed in women's basketball than men's but growing rapidly.

The Roster Decision Intelligence engine accounts for all of these dynamics when recommending portal targets.

## 24. Core Governance Rules

1. Deterministic: Same inputs produce same outputs.
2. Auditable: Every step logged with inputs, outputs, confidence.
3. No truth mutation: Downstream engines never modify upstream outputs.
4. Confidence transparency: Every output includes confidence_pct.
5. No data fabrication: Missing data = UNSCORED, never guessed.
6. Legend is display-only: Legends interpret KR, they do not produce it.
7. KLVN normalization: All cross-level comparisons use lambdas.

## 25. The Legend Anchor Rule

The legend anchor is truth. The math is confirmation. Not the other way around.

Phase 3 (production anchor against the women's legend) sets the floor and ceiling for the final KR. Phase 6 (component KR math) adjusts within that range +/- 10. If the math produces a number outside the anchor range by more than 10, the trait scores need re-examination, not the anchor.

Anchor against production profile numbers, not award labels. Awards confirm. Production determines.

## 26. What the System Does Not Do

- Does not compare women's KR to men's KR across genders
- Does not predict or assume pregnancy or family planning
- Does not apply men's physical benchmarks to women's players
- Does not use pre-2021-22 shooting data without line-distance adjustment
- Does not fabricate data when it is missing
- Does not allow downstream engines to modify upstream truth
- Does not produce editorial opinions, marketing language, or subjective rankings
- Does not project high school or AAU players who have no college production data
