# Banking Infrastructure Strategy

**KaNeXT LLC | Confidential**

---

## Purpose

This document explains how the financial rails behind KPay are structured, the phased approach from third-party banking-as-a-service to owned bank charter, and the Year 2+ bank acquisition capital event thesis. Banking infrastructure is a Year 2+ concern, not a Year 1 priority. KPay operates from Day 1 on a BaaS partnership. An owned bank charter is a future capital event raised separately from the Year 1 $33M partnership raise.

---

## The Strategic Case for Owning a Charter

KPay is the financial layer of the KaNeXT OS. Every transaction on the platform - tuition, housing, dining, merch, tickets, donations, NIL, remittance, concessions - flows through KPay.

Without an owned charter, KPay operates through a white-label banking-as-a-service (BaaS) partner. The BaaS partner holds the charter, controls the terms, sets the fees, and can change or terminate the relationship. KaNeXT captures the software margin but not the banking margin. The BaaS partner sits between KaNeXT and the financial infrastructure that powers every transaction on the platform.

With an owned charter, KaNeXT controls the financial infrastructure directly. Tuition processing, financial aid disbursement, payroll, vendor payments, remittance, lending, deposit management, and institutional capital flows all move through infrastructure KaNeXT owns. Dependency on a single third-party partner is eliminated. The banking margin (deposit spread, lending income, interchange) is captured internally rather than shared with a BaaS provider.

At the transaction volumes the mandate and institutional network are designed to produce, the difference between BaaS margin and owned-charter margin is substantial recurring revenue.

---

## Phase 1: BaaS (Year 1 and Early Year 2)

KPay does not require an owned charter to launch. Phase 1 financial operations run through a BaaS partnership.

**Wallet operations.** KPay wallets activate through a BaaS partner. Students, parents, fans, and institutional users hold balances, make transactions, and receive payments through the wallet. The user experience is identical whether the underlying charter is owned or partnered.

**Commerce processing.** Flat processing on all institutional transactions. Card-present and card-not-present. Wallet-to-wallet closed-loop transactions. Standard card-network processing infrastructure handles the underlying rails.

**Basic remittance.** International transfers through partnered remittance infrastructure. Lower fees than legacy providers but not yet at the full margin capture of an owned charter.

**Institutional financial operations.** Tuition payment, financial aid disbursement, campus commerce, dining, parking: all processing through KPay from Day 1, backed by the BaaS charter.

The BaaS phase is fully functional. It is not a degraded experience. The difference is margin capture and strategic independence, not user-facing capability.

## Phase 2: Bank Acquisition (Year 2+ Capital Event)

The bank acquisition is a Year 2+ capital event raised separately from the Year 1 partnership funding. Acquisition capital is sized and structured at the time of the capital event based on Year 1 operational results, the specific acquisition target's condition at that time, and regulatory conditions.

### The Acquisition Target

KaNeXT has identified a Miami-based community bank as the primary acquisition target. The target is FDIC-insured, federally chartered, and carries several structural characteristics that make it an unusually good fit: sub-$100M asset base with branch infrastructure in Miami-Dade County, distressed ownership profile that creates a motivated-seller dynamic, and a regulatory posture that allows a clean change-of-control path. Additional details on the specific target, including operational metrics, ownership structure, and litigation context, are available under a non-disclosure framework during diligence conversations.

The target's distressed position creates the acquisition opportunity. The ownership profile creates the motivation for a clean exit. KaNeXT's deposit commitments, regulatory cleanup, and structural rebuild create the value the seller cannot create alone. The specifics of the target are withheld from this summary document to preserve negotiating posture and because naming identifying details publicly would compromise the acquisition.

### Acquisition Structure

A nominal upfront purchase consideration structure may be feasible if deposit commitments, regulatory approval, and transaction cleanup provide sufficient value to the current ownership.

KaNeXT would commit approximately $50M to $100M in new deposits through its institutional network, including the partner institution institutional and student accounts under the Full IOA, KPay wallet balances across the broader platform user base, mandate institution commerce settlement through GAAC and expanded governing body partnerships, remittance float through cross-border corridors, and fund and institutional capital flows.

The current ownership receives a clean exit. KaNeXT receives a fully chartered, FDIC-insured national bank with existing branch infrastructure, lending capabilities, and regulatory approvals.

The specific terms of any acquisition are subject to negotiation, regulatory approval, and completion of due diligence.

### Post-Acquisition Plan

**Rebrand.** The existing brand carries reputational baggage from the ownership litigation. A clean rebrand signals new ownership, new direction, and alignment with the KaNeXT institutional ecosystem.

**Apply for MDI designation.** The acquired institution may qualify for Minority Depository Institution status through minority governance composition and community-service criteria. MDI designation is administered by the FDIC based on actual ownership, governance, and community-service profile. KaNeXT's founder composition and planned majority-minority board structure provide a credible MDI path independent of any specific institutional partnership. If MDI status is granted, it activates federal capital programs, corporate deposit flows, and CRA-motivated lending from major banks.

**Reconstitute the board.** Current board members associated with the existing ownership structure will be replaced with a majority-minority board appointed by KaNeXT. Board composition will satisfy both MDI governance requirements and KaNeXT's institutional operating standards.

**Capitalize.** Approximately $10M in initial capital. This is a balance sheet asset (it sits in the bank's capital account), not a sunk cost. It expands the bank's lending capacity and satisfies regulatory capital requirements for the planned growth trajectory. Additional capital may be accessible through federal capital programs discussed below.

**Integrate with KPay.** The bank charter provides the regulated depository infrastructure underlying KPay wallet operations. Student accounts, institutional deposits, remittance processing, and settlement activity transition from the BaaS partner to the owned charter. The user experience does not change. The margin capture changes substantially.

**Pursue CDFI certification.** Community Development Financial Institutions Fund certification is pursued if the institution's post-close business model, target-market focus, and community accountability satisfy the certification requirements. CDFI certification opens additional federal funding programs including CDFI Fund Financial Assistance awards and the Bond Guarantee Program.

**Evaluate federal capital programs.** Programs such as the Emergency Capital Investment Program have historically provided capital to qualifying institutions at favorable terms. ECIP's original application window closed in January 2023 but illustrates the type of federal capital program that can materially improve bank economics. Future programs or reopened windows are pursued as they become available.

**Open branches at institutional network campuses.** the partner institution's South Florida campus and the Miami Lakes flagship (upon development) become branch locations. Each institution added to the KaNeXT institutional network over time represents a potential branch opportunity inside a captive institutional environment with an embedded, structurally recurring account base.

## Regulatory Process

### FDIC Change of Control

The acquisition requires FDIC approval through the change-of-control application process. The FDIC evaluates the acquiring party's financial capacity, management competence, compliance history, and the impact on the institution's safety and soundness.

**Pre-filing meeting.** Engage the FDIC and OCC (for national charters) before submitting the formal application. The pre-filing meeting identifies potential concerns and shapes the application.

**Application submission.** Formal change-of-control application with supporting documentation: acquirer financial statements, business plan, management team qualifications, deposit growth projections, capital plan, and compliance infrastructure.

**Review timeline.** 6-12 months minimum from application to approval. Distressed ownership situations can materially extend the timeline if the regulator requires additional diligence, conditions the approval on full resolution of outstanding matters, or requires enhanced compliance commitments as a condition of transfer. The target's specific profile is evaluated against these factors during the pre-filing engagement.

**Conditions.** The FDIC may impose conditions on approval: enhanced compliance monitoring, capital maintenance requirements, business plan milestones, or periodic reporting. Conditions are manageable and expected for a change-of-control transaction involving a distressed institution.

### State and Federal Coordination

The target bank is OCC-regulated as a national charter, making the OCC the primary regulator. MDI designation is administered by the FDIC. CDFI certification is administered by the Treasury Department's CDFI Fund. Each application path runs in parallel post-closing.

## Deposit Growth Trajectory

Deposits are generated structurally across the institutional network, platform user base, governing body mandate, and fund capital flows.

**Institutional Network:** the partner institution student wallet accounts, faculty and staff accounts, institutional operating accounts, payroll deposits, and tuition flow. Each institution added to the network over time adds a complete set of institutional and individual deposit accounts.

**Governing Body Mandate:** commerce settlement deposits across mandate institutions, growing as governing body agreements are executed and transaction volume scales.

**Platform:** KPay wallet balances from all users regardless of institutional affiliation, remittance float from international transfers, and interchange settlement.

**Fund and Institutional Capital:** fund capital under management, management fee flow, philanthropic capital in institutional accounts, and future institutional capital events.

**CRA Corporate Deposits:** major banks depositing into the MDI to satisfy regulatory obligations, independent of KaNeXT's own customer acquisition, upon MDI designation.

Estimated deposit trajectory:

| Year | Estimated Deposits | Primary Sources |
|---|---|---|
| Y1 | n/a | BaaS phase, no owned charter |
| Y2 | $50M-$75M (post-acquisition) | the partner institution accounts, fund capital, early wallet adoption, initial mandate commerce |
| Y3 | $150M-$250M | Institutional network expansion, growing wallet base, CRA deposits, remittance float |
| Y4 | $300M-$500M | Mandate fully deployed, significant wallet adoption, lending portfolio growth |
| Y5 | $500M-$1B+ | Mature institutional network, full mandate commerce, diaspora remittance scale |

These estimates assume Year 2 acquisition execution. Actual results depend on acquisition timing, regulatory approval, mandate deployment pace, institutional network expansion, and wallet adoption rates.

## Revenue Streams (Post-Charter)

Revenue is generated through five primary channels once the owned charter is operational.

**Deposit spread income.** The difference between what the bank earns on deposits (federal funds rate or equivalent) and what it pays wallet holders. Pure margin that scales linearly with deposit volume.

**Lending income.** Student loans, SBA-guaranteed loans, commercial lending, real estate lending (cluster housing and Miami Lakes development), and personal lending. Platform-native underwriting data (transaction history, income patterns, account tenure) enables better credit decisions than traditional banks can make without this data.

**Interchange income.** 1-2% on every KaNeXT Card transaction outside the ecosystem (Apple Pay, Google Pay, physical card). Monetizes every dollar a user spends anywhere.

**Treasury and institutional services.** Cash management, payroll processing, accounts payable for institutions on the platform. Each institution becomes a corporate banking client.

**CRA-motivated corporate deposits.** Major banks seeking Community Reinvestment Act credit deposit funds into MDI-designated banks at favorable terms upon MDI designation. These deposits expand the deposit base without marketing cost.

**Transaction processing.** Every KPay settlement event generates processing revenue. At mandate-scale transaction volumes, this is substantial recurring revenue.

Bank revenue is excluded from the Institutional Revenue Model. All bank revenue is additional upside.

## Fallback: If the Acquisition Fails

If the specific target bank cannot be acquired (regulatory denial, ownership cannot reach agreement, diligence reveals disqualifying issues), KaNeXT has multiple alternative paths.

**Alternative acquisition target.** There are approximately 150 MDI-designated banks in the United States and additional distressed-seller community banks in the broader market. The acquisition intelligence system evaluates alternative targets against the same criteria.

**De novo charter application.** Apply for a new bank charter through the OCC (national) or state regulator. De novo charter applications are more time-intensive (18-36 months) but provide a clean start without legacy issues. De novo applications have increased in recent years as regulators have become more receptive.

**Extended BaaS period.** Continue operating KPay through the BaaS partnership for an extended period. This is the default if neither acquisition nor de novo charter is achievable on the desired timeline. KPay remains fully functional. The margin capture is lower but the user experience and platform economics continue.

The bank charter is strategically important but not operationally blocking. KPay launches and operates on Day 1 through BaaS regardless of charter status. The charter improves economics. It does not enable them.

## Due Diligence Priorities (At Time of Acquisition)

Regulatory standing: current federal examination status, any outstanding consent orders, enforcement actions, or compliance deficiencies. Any ownership-related legal or regulatory exposure must be fully understood and resolved or mitigated prior to closing.

Asset quality: loan portfolio composition, non-performing asset ratios, and loss reserves. Any material contingent liabilities must be identified during due diligence.

Operational infrastructure: core banking systems, compliance architecture, staffing, and technology. Integration cost and timeline with KPay must be assessed prior to closing.

Regulatory approval path: OCC and FDIC change-of-control approval timeline, any conditions or commitments required, and MDI designation eligibility confirmation.
