## 1. CANDIDATE KR - UNIVERSAL RATING (0-100)

### 1.1 Purpose

Candidate KR is a single universal number (0-100) that represents the overall quality of a candidate relative to the role they are being evaluated for. It is the hiring equivalent of Player KR in the basketball intelligence system.

Candidate KR is computed from four component KRs, weighted by role type. The component KR weights vary by role because different roles demand different capability mixes.

### 1.2 Component KRs

#### CKR - Competency KR

Measures technical and domain capability for the specific role.

Inputs:
- Technical skills directly relevant to the role (demonstrated, not claimed)
- Domain expertise depth (years in the specific field, not adjacent fields)
- Relevant experience (roles that directly prepared them for this one)
- Education and credentials (degrees, certifications, licenses - weighted by role relevance)
- Demonstrated results (quantifiable outcomes from prior roles)
- Industry knowledge (understanding of the sector, competitive landscape, regulatory environment)

Scoring:
- 90-100: Elite domain expert. Top of field. Published, recognized, or demonstrated at the highest level. Results that materially moved organizations.
- 80-89: Strong expert. Deep domain knowledge with clear demonstrated results. Could lead a function at this level.
- 70-79: Solid practitioner. Competent across core requirements. Some gaps in depth or breadth but functional.
- 60-69: Developing. Meets minimum requirements. Gaps in either experience or demonstrated results. Needs mentorship.
- 50-59: Below threshold. Significant gaps. Would require substantial investment to reach competency.
- Below 50: Does not meet minimum qualifications.

#### LKR - Leadership KR

Measures leadership capability, management capacity, and organizational influence.

Inputs:
- Management experience (people managed, scope of responsibility, budget authority)
- Team building track record (built teams from scratch vs inherited, retention rates, team outcomes)
- Decision-making quality (evidence of sound judgment under uncertainty, speed of decision-making)
- Communication skills (written, verbal, presentation - assessed from work product, references, interviews)
- Conflict resolution (evidence of navigating organizational tension, difficult conversations, realignment)
- Strategic thinking (ability to connect tactical work to institutional goals, long-term planning evidence)
- Ability to operate under constraint (doing more with less, startup-like conditions, resource scarcity)

Scoring:
- 90-100: Organizational leader. Can run an institution. Builds culture, develops leaders, makes bet-the-company decisions correctly. Rare.
- 80-89: Strong leader. Can run a department or major function. Develops people. Navigates complexity. Trusted to operate autonomously.
- 70-79: Effective manager. Runs a team well. Communicates clearly. Makes good decisions within defined scope. Needs strategic direction from above.
- 60-69: Developing leader. Can manage a small team with oversight. Decision-making is inconsistent under pressure. Growing.
- 50-59: Individual contributor. Leadership is aspirational, not demonstrated. May have potential but no track record.
- Below 50: No leadership evidence. Not a disqualifier for IC roles.

#### FKR - Fit KR

Measures alignment with KaNeXT institutional values, department culture, and operating environment.

Inputs:
- Alignment with KaNeXT institutional values (Christian identity, excellence, innovation, accountability)
- Alignment with specific department culture (coaching staff culture differs from technology team culture)
- Alignment with direct manager's operating style (communication cadence, autonomy level, feedback style)
- Geographic willingness (relocation to Miami, travel requirements, campus presence expectations)
- Compensation expectations alignment (within institutional pay bands, equity/upside understanding)

Scoring:
- 90-100: Natural alignment. Values match. Culture match. Geographic match. Compensation match. This person was built for this environment.
- 80-89: Strong fit. Minor friction points (geographic hesitation, slight comp gap, different communication style) but fundamentally aligned.
- 70-79: Workable fit. Some real friction. May need culture onboarding. Compensation negotiation required. But core values align.
- 60-69: Questionable fit. Multiple friction points. Values alignment unclear. May struggle in startup-intensity environment. High risk of early departure.
- 50-59: Poor fit. Fundamental misalignment on values, expectations, or operating style. Hire at significant risk.
- Below 50: Incompatible. Do not hire regardless of competency.

#### GKR - Growth KR

Measures trajectory, learning capacity, and development ceiling.

Inputs:
- Learning velocity (how quickly they acquire new skills, adapt to new domains - evidence from career transitions)
- Adaptability (career pivots, industry changes, role expansions - did they succeed or struggle)
- Career trajectory (ascending, plateaued, or descending - based on responsibility growth over time)
- Coachability (response to feedback, evidence of incorporating direction, self-correction)
- Self-awareness (accurate assessment of own strengths and weaknesses - measured in interviews)
- Ambition level relative to role scope (overqualified candidates with ceiling ambition are flight risks; underambitious candidates plateau)

Scoring:
- 90-100: Exceptional trajectory. Rapid ascent. Multiple successful transitions. Learns anything fast. Will outgrow most roles quickly. Best suited for roles with expansion built in.
- 80-89: Strong growth. Clear upward trajectory. Adapts well. Coachable. 2-3 year runway before needing larger scope.
- 70-79: Steady growth. Improving but at a normal pace. Will develop within role. Not going to surprise you.
- 60-69: Plateauing. Growth has slowed. May be at their level. Not necessarily negative - some roles need stability, not growth.
- 50-59: Stagnant. No evidence of growth in recent years. May have peaked. Concerning for roles that require adaptation.
- Below 50: Declining. Career trajectory is downward. Results are getting worse, not better.

### 1.3 Component KR Weights by Role Type

| Role Type | CKR | LKR | FKR | GKR |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| C-Suite/Executive | 25% | 35% | 25% | 15% |
| Head Coach | 30% | 25% | 25% | 20% |
| Assistant Coach | 35% | 15% | 25% | 25% |
| Faculty (tenure-track) | 45% | 15% | 20% | 20% |
| Faculty (adjunct) | 50% | 10% | 25% | 15% |
| Admissions/Enrollment | 30% | 20% | 30% | 20% |
| Campus Operations | 35% | 20% | 30% | 15% |
| Technology/Engineering | 45% | 15% | 20% | 20% |
| Administrative/Support | 30% | 15% | 35% | 20% |

Rationale:
- C-Suite weights Leadership highest because executives are hired to lead, not execute
- Head Coaches weight Competency highest because coaching knowledge is non-negotiable, but Leadership and Fit are nearly equal because culture building and institutional alignment define long-term success
- Faculty (tenure-track) weights Competency highest because academic credentials and research output are gatekeeping requirements
- Admissions weights Fit highest among non-support roles because enrollment staff ARE the institutional brand to prospective students
- Administrative/Support weights Fit highest because these roles require deep cultural integration and are often the most visible daily touchpoints for students and families

## 3. KLVN NORMALIZATION

### 3.1 Purpose

KLVN (Kalejaiye Level Value Normalization) adjusts inputs during competency scoring to ensure fair cross-context comparison. A Director-level achievement at a Fortune 500 company and a Director-level achievement at a 10-person startup are different accomplishments in different contexts. KLVN normalizes the environment so the candidate's underlying capability is assessed fairly.

KLVN normalizes INPUTS, not OUTPUTS. The final Candidate KR is a universal number. There is no "Fortune 500-equivalent KR" or "startup-equivalent KR."

### 3.2 Lambda by Previous Employer Tier

| Employer Tier | Lambda | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Fortune 100 / Global Institution | 0.85 | Achievements happened within massive infrastructure, large teams, and institutional momentum. A "success" at this level often had significant organizational support. Slightly discount to isolate individual contribution. |
| Fortune 500 / Major University / Power Conference Athletic Dept | 0.90 | Still significant institutional support but individual contribution is more visible. |
| Mid-Market / Regional Institution / Mid-Major Athletic Dept | 1.00 | Baseline. This is the normalization anchor. |
| Small Company / Small Institution / Low-Major or D2 Athletic Dept | 1.10 | Achievements required more individual effort with fewer resources. Slightly amplify. |
| Startup / NAIA / NJCAA / USCAA / Under-Resourced Program | 1.20 | Achievements happened with minimal infrastructure, budget, staff, and institutional support. Significant amplification to capture individual capability that was suppressed by environment. |
| Solo / Freelance / Volunteer | 1.25 | Maximum amplification. Producing results with zero organizational support is the strongest signal of individual capability. |

### 3.3 Lambda by Career Stage

| Career Stage | Lambda | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Entry-Level (0-2 years) | 1.15 | Early-career achievements carry extra weight because they happened with minimal experience and network. |
| Early Career (3-5 years) | 1.05 | Slight amplification. Still building capability. |
| Mid-Career (6-12 years) | 1.00 | Baseline. Expected to produce results at this stage. |
| Senior Career (13-20 years) | 0.95 | Slight discount. Achievements should be larger and more impactful at this stage. Same-sized results carry less weight. |
| Late Career (20+ years) | 0.90 | Results expected to compound. An executive with 25 years of experience should have more to show than one with 10 years. |

### 3.4 Lambda by Industry Transition

| Transition Type | Lambda | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Same industry, same role type | 1.00 | No adjustment. Direct comparison. |
| Same industry, different role type | 1.05 | Slight amplification for role adaptation. |
| Adjacent industry, same role type | 1.10 | Industry knowledge gap requires adaptation. Competency may be temporarily suppressed by unfamiliar domain. |
| Different industry, same role type | 1.15 | Significant domain shift. Transferable skills exist but domain learning curve is real. |
| Different industry, different role type | 1.20 | Double transition. Maximum amplification for career changers demonstrating transferable capability. |

### 3.5 Composite Lambda

Composite Lambda = Employer_Lambda x Career_Stage_Lambda x Industry_Transition_Lambda

Applied to: CKR inputs during trait scoring.

NOT applied to: LKR, FKR, or GKR (these are assessed independently of employer context).

## 4. SYSTEM FIT - INSTITUTIONAL CULTURE FIT

### 4.1 Purpose

System Fit measures how well a candidate aligns with the KaNeXT institutional operating environment. This is not a personality test. It is a structural assessment of whether the candidate can thrive in the specific conditions they will face.

System Fit contributes to FKR (Fit KR) computation.

### 4.2 System Fit Dimensions

#### Operating Style Fit

Assessment: Does this person thrive in startup environments or established institutions?

Indicators (startup-fit positive):
- Has built something from nothing
- Has operated with ambiguous job descriptions
- Has worn multiple hats simultaneously
- Has succeeded without established processes
- References describe them as "self-starting" or "entrepreneurial"

Indicators (institutional-fit, startup-risk):
- Career is entirely within large, established organizations
- Has always had clear role boundaries and process
- Struggles when asked about ambiguity or resource scarcity
- References describe them as "follows process" without evidence of process creation

Scoring: 0-100 sub-score feeding into FKR.

#### Faith Alignment

Assessment: Can this person operate comfortably within a Christian institutional framework?

This is NOT a religious test. It assesses comfort and alignment, not belief. The question is whether the candidate can operate within an institutional culture that includes prayer, chapel, faith-based programming, and Christian values without friction.

Indicators (aligned):
- Experience at faith-based institutions
- Personal comfort with faith-based workplace culture
- Understands and respects the role of faith in institutional identity
- Does not require a secular workplace

Indicators (misaligned):
- Explicit discomfort with faith-based institutional culture
- History of conflict with faith-based policies or norms
- Requires secular workplace

Scoring: 0-100 sub-score feeding into FKR. A score below 60 is a hard block on hiring regardless of other scores.

#### Autonomy Level

Assessment: Can this person operate with high autonomy and minimal supervision?

This is critical for Year 1. There is no deep management layer. People must self-direct, self-prioritize, and deliver without handholding.

Indicators (high autonomy):
- Has managed their own workload without a manager
- Has prioritized competing demands and delivered
- References describe them as "didn't need to be managed"
- Has produced results in environments with minimal structure

Indicators (low autonomy):
- Has always had a direct manager providing daily direction
- Struggles with ambiguity or competing priorities
- References describe needing "clear expectations" or "structured guidance"

Scoring: 0-100 sub-score feeding into FKR.

#### Pressure Tolerance

Assessment: Can this person perform under the intensity of building an institution from near-zero?

Indicators (high tolerance):
- Has worked in high-pressure environments (startup, turnaround, competitive athletics, medical, military)
- References describe grace under pressure
- Has met aggressive deadlines without quality collapse
- Has managed crisis situations

Indicators (low tolerance):
- Career has been in stable, low-pressure environments
- References describe stress-related performance drops
- Interview reveals anxiety about pace, workload, or ambiguity

Scoring: 0-100 sub-score feeding into FKR.

#### Multi-Role Flexibility

Assessment: Is this person willing and able to operate outside their strict job description?

Year 1 requires everyone to do everything. The CFO will help move furniture. The admissions director will staff game-day operations. The head coach will recruit, fundraise, and speak at chapel.

Indicators (flexible):
- Has operated across multiple functions
- Willing to do whatever is needed
- References describe "rolled up their sleeves"
- Career shows breadth, not just depth

Indicators (rigid):
- "That's not my job" attitude
- Career is narrowly scoped
- Uncomfortable with tasks outside their expertise
- References describe needing clear role boundaries

Scoring: 0-100 sub-score feeding into FKR.

### 4.3 System Fit Composite

System_Fit = (Operating_Style x 0.25) + (Faith_Alignment x 0.20) + (Autonomy x 0.25) + (Pressure_Tolerance x 0.15) + (Multi_Role x 0.15)

System Fit feeds directly into FKR computation. It is the primary driver of FKR alongside geographic and compensation alignment.

### 4.4 System Fit Hard Blocks

These result in automatic disqualification regardless of other scores:
- Faith Alignment below 60
- Autonomy Level below 50 (for Year 1 hires; relaxes to below 40 after Year 3 when management layers exist)
- Pressure Tolerance below 50 (for Year 1 hires; relaxes to below 40 after Year 3)

## 5. COACHING STAFF SPECIFIC

### 5.1 Purpose

Coaching staff evaluation is the deepest section of Hiring Intelligence because KaNeXT is hiring coaches across up to 13 sports at up to 4 levels each (Varsity, JV1, JV2, Prep Academy). At full scale, this is 52+ teams with 150-200+ coaching positions. Every coaching hire must be evaluated against both the Hiring Intelligence framework AND the relevant sports intelligence system.

### 5.2 Coaching Staff Component KRs (Override)

For Head Coach and Assistant Coach candidates, the standard CKR/LKR/FKR/GKR components are supplemented with coaching-specific sub-scores:

#### Coaching Philosophy Alignment (feeds CKR)

Assessment: Does their offensive and defensive system identity match what KaNeXT wants to build?

Process:
1. Identify the candidate's primary offensive system (from their coaching history, clinics, film study)
2. Identify the candidate's primary defensive system
3. Compare against the target system identity for the program
4. Score alignment: Perfect match = 95+, Compatible = 80-94, Adaptable = 65-79, Misaligned = below 65

For basketball: Cross-reference the 12 offensive systems and 10 defensive systems defined in the basketball intelligence SKILL.md. A candidate who runs Princeton offense being hired for a program that wants Dribble Drive is a system identity mismatch that must be flagged.

For other sports: System identity definitions will be built per sport as the intelligence system expands.

#### System Identity (feeds CKR)

Assessment: What system do they actually run? Not what they claim - what does their game film and history show?

Inputs:
- Film review of their teams' games
- Play design samples
- Practice plan samples
- Clinic presentations
- Former player and assistant testimony
- Win/loss record within their system (did the system produce results or just look good on paper)

This requires the same level of rigor as evaluating a player's production. Coaches claim systems they don't actually run. The evidence must be in the results.

#### Recruiting Network Value (feeds CKR)

Assessment: How valuable is this coach's recruiting network to the program?

Dimensions:
- Geographic reach (local, regional, national, international)
- Level of contacts (HS coaches, club coaches, AAU directors, prep school administrators, international scouts)
- Relationship quality (do recruits actually take their calls, or is the network theoretical)
- Pipeline access (do they have access to feeder programs that consistently produce talent)
- International pipeline (critical for KaNeXT - access to African, Caribbean, European, Asian talent pipelines)
- Portal/transfer relationships (for college coaches - do they have relationships with players and coaches at other programs)

Scoring method: Web search for the coach's recruiting classes, verbal commitments they were primary on, recruiting rankings of their programs. Cross-reference against the competitive level.

#### Player Development Track Record (feeds CKR)

Assessment: How many players have they developed to the next level?

Inputs:
- Players who advanced from their program to a higher competitive level
- Players who improved measurably (stats, KR, role expansion) under their coaching
- Players who earned all-conference, all-region, all-American under their coaching
- Players who earned professional contracts from their program
- Development timeline (did players improve quickly or was it a slow grind)

For basketball: Cross-reference against the basketball intelligence system. If a coach's program has been evaluated with Team KR, use the team evaluation data.

#### Compliance History (feeds FKR)

Assessment: Any NCAA/NAIA/NJCAA violations, program sanctions, or personal compliance issues?

Inputs:
- Web search for coach name + violations/sanctions/NCAA/investigation
- Reference checks specifically asking about compliance
- Employment history gaps that may indicate forced departure

Scoring: Clean record = no penalty. Minor violation (secondary, self-reported) = FKR -5. Major violation (Level I/II, program sanctions) = FKR -15 to -25 depending on severity. Active investigation = evaluation paused until resolution.

#### Retention and Culture (feeds FKR)

Assessment: Do players stay? Do they transfer out? What do former players say?

Inputs:
- Transfer rate from the program (abnormally high transfer-out rate is a red flag)
- Player retention across eligibility (do players stay all 4 years or leave early via portal)
- Former player testimony (public statements, social media, reference calls)
- Assistant coach retention (do assistants stay or constantly turn over)
- Public culture indicators (team social media presence, community engagement, academic performance)

### 5.3 Coaching Staff KR Computation

For Head Coaches:
Candidate KR = (CKR_Enhanced x 0.30) + (LKR x 0.25) + (FKR_Enhanced x 0.25) + (GKR x 0.20)

Where CKR_Enhanced includes: base CKR + Coaching Philosophy Alignment + System Identity + Recruiting Network + Player Development Track Record (equally weighted within CKR)

Where FKR_Enhanced includes: base FKR + Compliance History + Retention and Culture

For Assistant Coaches:
Candidate KR = (CKR_Enhanced x 0.35) + (LKR x 0.15) + (FKR_Enhanced x 0.25) + (GKR x 0.25)

Assistant Coaches weight CKR higher (skill development and recruiting are paramount) and GKR higher (growth trajectory matters more because you're developing future head coaches) while weighting LKR lower (they don't need to lead the program yet).

### 5.4 Sport-Specific Considerations

#### Revenue Sports (Football, Men's Basketball, Women's Basketball)
- Higher weight on Recruiting Network (these sports are the enrollment and revenue engines)
- National-level recruiting contacts expected for HC
- System identity alignment is non-negotiable (the athletic system identity must be coherent across levels)
- Player development to professional level is a differentiator

#### Olympic Sports (Soccer, Volleyball, Track, Baseball, Softball, etc.)
- Higher weight on Player Development (fewer recruiting resources means development is the primary growth lever)
- Regional recruiting network acceptable (national is a bonus)
- Dual-sport awareness valued (understanding how athletes may participate across sports)

#### Development Levels (JV1, JV2, Prep Academy)
- Highest weight on Player Development and Growth KR (these coaches develop raw athletes)
- Lower weight on Recruiting Network (varsity staff recruits, development staff develops)
- Patience and teaching ability are critical CKR components
- Direct pipeline to varsity coaching staff is a fit requirement (development coaches must communicate seamlessly with varsity staff about player readiness)

## 6. SUPPRESSION DETECTION

### 6.1 Purpose

KaNeXT's institutional thesis is that talent is universally distributed but opportunity is not. The same principle applies to hiring. Exceptional candidates are systematically overlooked, underutilized, or undervalued because of the environments they've been in. Suppression detection is MANDATORY in every candidate evaluation.

### 6.2 Suppression Types

#### Environment Suppression

Definition: A great candidate was at a bad organization. Their results were suppressed by the environment, not their ability.

Indicators:
- Candidate's organization was objectively dysfunctional (layoffs, leadership turnover, budget cuts, toxic culture, regulatory problems)
- The candidate's role was eliminated or reduced due to organizational issues, not performance
- Colleagues at the same organization also underperformed relative to their known capability
- The candidate achieved notable results DESPITE the dysfunction (this is the strongest signal)

Example: A basketball assistant coach at a program with no recruiting budget, no facilities, and a toxic head coach who drives players away. Their "results" (3 players placed at the next level in 5 years) look mediocre - but given the environment, they're exceptional.

Adjustment: When environment suppression is detected, compute a Suppression Uplift range. This is the estimated delta between observed performance and projected performance in a functional environment.

Typical uplift: +5 to +15 KR points, depending on severity of suppression.

#### Role Suppression

Definition: The candidate was underutilized in their previous role. An assistant who should have been a head coach. A senior engineer doing junior work. A department head doing administrative tasks.

Indicators:
- Title/role mismatch with actual capabilities observed in interviews or work product
- References say "they were the best person on the team but never got the title"
- Career progression stalled at a specific organization while peers advanced
- Evidence of performing above their role (leading projects, mentoring, making decisions that were above their pay grade)

Example: A volunteer assistant basketball coach who is functionally the recruiting coordinator and lead skill developer but carries no title, no salary, and no credit. This is the Lincoln University pattern - the person is performing a role two levels above their position.

Adjustment: Typical uplift: +5 to +10 KR points.

#### Resource Suppression

Definition: The candidate produced results with no budget, no staff, and no infrastructure. Their output was constrained by resources, not capability.

Indicators:
- Small team with large output (one person doing the work of three)
- No budget achievements (grew enrollment/revenue/results without marketing spend or equipment investment)
- Manual processes (built systems by hand that larger organizations automate)
- Geographic isolation (produced results in a market with limited talent pipeline, limited visibility, limited institutional support)

Example: A head coach at a USCAA program with zero scholarships, a $5K recruiting budget, and no dedicated gym who still won conference championships and placed players at D2/NAIA programs. This is elite coaching suppressed by zero resources.

Adjustment: Typical uplift: +8 to +15 KR points. Resource suppression is the strongest suppression signal.

#### Bias Suppression

Definition: The candidate was overlooked due to age, gender, race, pedigree, school name, or other factors unrelated to capability.

Indicators:
- Career history shows consistent underpromotion relative to results
- Comparable peers with similar or weaker results advanced faster
- The candidate's profile doesn't fit the "typical" hire for their field (HBCU graduate in a field that defaults to Ivy League, woman in a male-dominated coaching sport, older candidate in a youth-biased tech role)
- References acknowledge the bias explicitly

This is the hardest suppression to quantify but the most important for KaNeXT's mission. The entire institutional thesis is that talent exists everywhere and is suppressed by broken systems.

Adjustment: Typical uplift: +3 to +10 KR points, depending on evidence strength.

### 6.3 Suppression Scoring Rules

1. Suppression types stack. A candidate can have environment + resource + bias suppression simultaneously.
2. Maximum total suppression uplift: +20 KR points. Beyond this, the evidence is insufficient or the candidate's ceiling is being inflated beyond defensible range.
3. Suppression must be evidenced, not assumed. Every suppression claim requires specific indicators from the evaluation data.
4. Suppression uplift is reported as a range, not a point estimate. "Candidate KR: 72, Suppression-Adjusted Range: 80-87."
5. Suppression-adjusted KR is the SECONDARY output. The raw Candidate KR (without suppression) is always reported first. Suppression adjustment is additive context, not a replacement.
6. Confidence on suppression-adjusted KR is always lower than on raw KR. The adjustment is an estimate.

## 7. HIRING RISK FLAGS

### 7.1 Purpose

Risk flags are candidate-level warning signals. They do not automatically disqualify. They require investigation and explanation.

### 7.2 Risk Flag Inventory

#### Job Hopping

Trigger: 3+ employer changes in 5 years without clear upward progression.

Investigation: Was each move upward (title, scope, compensation)? Were moves involuntary (layoffs, relocations)? Does the candidate explain the pattern coherently?

Severity:
- Pattern with upward moves: No risk. This is career optimization.
- Pattern with lateral moves: Moderate risk. May indicate dissatisfaction pattern or inability to commit.
- Pattern with no clear logic: High risk. Flight risk is near-certain.

#### Termination History

Trigger: Any involuntary separation in the last 10 years.

Investigation: Fired for cause vs laid off vs restructured vs mutual separation. What does the candidate say? What do references say? Do the stories match?

Severity:
- Layoff/restructuring: Low risk if consistent with organizational context.
- Performance termination: High risk. Must be explained and contextualized. Check for environment suppression (was the termination actually a cultural mismatch at a toxic organization?).
- Termination for cause (misconduct, compliance): Very high risk. May be a hard block depending on the nature.

#### Reference Gaps

Trigger: The candidate cannot or will not provide references from their most recent 2 employers.

Investigation: Is there a legitimate reason (NDA, company policy, ongoing litigation)? Can they provide peer references instead of supervisor references? Does the gap correspond to a known negative event?

Severity:
- Recent employer only, with explanation: Low risk.
- Multiple employers, no explanation: High risk. What are they hiding?

#### Compensation Misalignment

Trigger: Candidate's compensation expectation is 150%+ of the role's market rate or institutional pay band.

Investigation: Is the expectation based on a legitimate prior comp history? Can equity, upside, or non-monetary benefits bridge the gap? Is the candidate willing to take a short-term reduction for long-term upside?

Severity:
- 150-175% of range with willingness to negotiate: Moderate risk. Negotiable.
- 175%+ with firm position: High risk. Likely won't accept or will resent the comp if they do.

#### Over-Qualification

Trigger: Candidate's experience and credentials significantly exceed the role requirements.

Investigation: Why do they want this role? Is it a lifestyle choice, a passion project, a stepping stone, or a desperation hire? Will they stay 3+ years or leave at the first better offer?

Severity:
- Clear motivation (mission alignment, geographic preference, career pivot): Low risk.
- No clear motivation: High risk. Will leave within 12 months.

#### Under-Qualification

Trigger: Candidate does not meet minimum requirements for 2+ core competency areas.

Investigation: Is there a suppression case? Are they a fast learner with transferable skills? Can the gap be closed within 90 days with training?

Severity:
- Gap closeable with training + mentorship: Moderate risk. Worth the investment if GKR is high.
- Gap is structural (missing credentials, missing fundamental experience): High risk. Do not hire into a role where failure is likely.

## 8. COMPENSATION INTELLIGENCE

### 8.1 Purpose

Compensation Intelligence provides market rate guidance, internal equity analysis, and total compensation structuring for every hire. It ensures KaNeXT pays fairly, competitively, and sustainably.

### 8.2 Market Rate Framework

Market rate analysis considers three layers:

Layer 1 - Geographic Market: Miami, FL metro area compensation data. Miami is a high-cost, high-growth market. Base rates are higher than national medians for most roles.

Layer 2 - Institutional Context: HBCU/faith-based/startup institution. This creates both compression (HBCU pay scales are historically below market for comparable roles at PWIs) and opportunity (mission-driven candidates may accept below-market base for mission alignment and upside).

Layer 3 - Role-Specific Market: Coaching salaries vary by sport, level, and division. Technology salaries are driven by the Miami tech market, not the education market. Executive salaries must be competitive with both higher-ed and private-sector equivalents.

### 8.3 Compensation Positioning Strategy

KaNeXT's compensation strategy varies by role criticality:

| Criticality Tier | Target Percentile | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Mission-Critical (C-Suite, Revenue Sport HC, Lead Engineers) | 75th-90th percentile | Must attract best available talent. Premium justified by institutional impact. |
| High-Impact (Department Heads, Assistant Coaches in revenue sports, Senior Faculty) | 60th-75th percentile | Competitive but not premium. Mission alignment and upside supplement base. |
| Core Staff (Faculty, Admissions, Operations, Technology) | 50th-65th percentile | Market rate. Benefits and culture are differentiators. |
| Support Staff (Administrative, Entry-Level, Student Workers) | 50th percentile | Fair market rate. Benefits, training, and growth opportunity are the draw. |

### 8.4 Total Compensation Components

Base Salary: Cash compensation. Benchmarked against market rate framework.

Housing Allowance: For campus-based staff (coaches, residence life, certain administrators). Particularly valuable in Miami's high housing cost environment.

Education Benefits: Tuition waiver for employee and dependents at FMU. Significant value proposition for faculty and staff with school-age children.

Health and Benefits: Standard institutional package. Medical, dental, vision, retirement match.

Performance Bonus: Available for roles with measurable outcomes (enrollment targets, win/loss, fundraising, engineering milestones). Structured as percentage of base, paid annually.

Equity/Upside: For executive and technology hires. KaNeXT equity or profit-sharing participation. This is the primary tool for attracting talent above the institutional pay scale. The pitch: "Base is below your market rate, but you're building something. The equity upside is significant if we execute."

Relocation: One-time relocation package for out-of-market hires. Scaled by role level.

### 8.5 Internal Equity Rules

1. No employee in the same role type and experience band can be paid more than 115% of the lowest-paid person in that band. This prevents pay inequity from compounding.
2. Compensation decisions are documented with market data justification. No off-book adjustments.
3. Coaching salaries within the same sport and level cannot vary by more than 20% unless justified by documented experience differential.
4. Annual compensation review is mandatory. Market data is refreshed annually.

## 9. DEAL STRUCTURE TEMPLATES (OFFER STRUCTURES)

### 9.1 Standard Full-Time Offer

Components: Base salary + benefits + PTO + education benefit. Standard for most institutional hires.

### 9.2 Executive Offer

Components: Base salary + benefits + performance bonus (15-30% of base) + equity/profit participation + housing allowance (if campus-based) + relocation. Used for C-Suite and senior leadership.

### 9.3 Coaching Offer

Components: Base salary + benefits + performance bonus (win targets, academic targets, recruiting targets) + camp/clinic revenue sharing + housing allowance (campus-based coaches) + education benefit. Multi-year term (2-4 years for HC, 1-2 years for assistants). Buyout clause (institution and coach). Non-compete for recruiting territory.

### 9.4 Faculty Offer

Components: Base salary + benefits + course load definition + research support (lab, travel, conference funding) + sabbatical eligibility (tenure-track only) + education benefit. Tenure-track: probationary period per institutional policy. Adjunct: per-course or per-semester contract.

### 9.5 Technology Offer

Components: Base salary + benefits + equity/profit participation + remote work flexibility (if applicable) + professional development budget + equipment allowance. Technology hires compete with Miami tech market, not education market. Equity is the primary differentiation tool.

### 9.6 Interim/Contract Offer

Components: Daily or monthly rate + defined scope + defined term + no benefits (or limited benefits). Used for: consultants, interim executives during search, specialized project hires, seasonal staff (game-day operations, camp staff).

## 10. INTEGRATION RISK FLAGS

### 10.1 Purpose

Integration risk flags assess post-hire organizational risk. These are separate from hiring risk flags (which assess candidate-level risk). Integration risks assess whether the organization can successfully absorb and integrate the new hire.

### 10.2 Integration Risk Inventory

#### Reporting Structure Ambiguity
Trigger: The new hire's reporting line is unclear or contested.
Risk: The hire will be frustrated, receive conflicting direction, or fall through organizational cracks.
Mitigation: Define reporting structure before offer. Document in offer letter.

#### Peer Resistance
Trigger: Existing team members have expressed concern about the hire or the role.
Risk: Political resistance undermines the new hire's ability to perform.
Mitigation: Address concerns before the hire starts. Set expectations with the team.

#### Resource Gap
Trigger: The role requires budget, tools, staff, or infrastructure that does not yet exist.
Risk: The hire cannot perform because the organization hasn't built the support structure.
Mitigation: Pre-build resources before start date or document a ramp timeline in the offer.

#### Culture Clash
Trigger: The hire comes from a fundamentally different organizational culture (corporate to startup, secular to faith-based, large to small).
Risk: The hire struggles to adapt and either leaves or underperforms.
Mitigation: Structured culture onboarding in the first 90 days. Assign a culture mentor.

#### Knowledge Transfer Dependency
Trigger: The hire replaces someone who has not documented their processes or relationships.
Risk: Critical institutional knowledge is lost.
Mitigation: Overlap period where possible. Documentation sprint before departure.
