**KaNeXT LLC | Confidential**

## Purpose

The Institutional Revenue Model projects enrollment growing from approximately 2,100 in Year 1 to 24,600-27,700 by Year 5. This document breaks down how those students are acquired: through which channels, at what conversion rates, at what cost, and under what governed intelligence. This is the operational detail behind the enrollment assumptions that drive the institutional revenue projections.

The model is not traditional enrollment marketing. The model is governed by the KaNeXT Enrollment Management Intelligence architecture, which operates across the full student lifecycle: acquisition, retention, persistence, and recovery. Enrollment forecasting, channel optimization, and retention outcomes are all tracked through the intelligence system. Every projection in this document is governed by the same architecture.

All figures are modeled estimates. Actual conversion rates, CAC, and channel mix depend on execution, market response, brand development, and competitive dynamics.

## Enrollment by Source

partner institution enrollment comes from four distinct channels. Each has different economics, different conversion profiles, and different scaling characteristics.

### Channel 1: Athletic Recruiting (On-Campus)

Athletes recruited through the intelligence system and the national player pool. This is the enrollment anchor in Year 1 and grows substantially as multi-level teams deploy across expanding sport coverage.

| Metric | Y1 | Y3 | Y5 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Varsity athletes | 150 | 350 | 500 |
| Development tier athletes | 70 | 200 | 400 |
| **Total athletic enrollment** | **220** | **550** | **900** |

The multi-level athletic structure is a major enrollment driver. Every Varsity team, every development tier team, and every additional sport added is filled with enrolled students paying tuition. The partner institution launches with six Varsity sports in Year 1 and scales to 14 sports by Year 5, each with Varsity plus at least one development tier. The athletic system alone contributes approximately 900 enrolled students by Year 5, generating continuous content for KTV, continuous visibility for the institutional brand, and continuous data for the intelligence system.

Conversion model: the intelligence system identifies athletes across the national player pool of 250,000+ evaluated athletes across every college level. Coaches use system fit, KR, and academic eligibility filters to build targeted prospect lists. Recruiting communication happens through the governed platform. Conversion from prospect to enrolled student depends on scholarship offer, NIL package, institutional fit, and competitive alternatives.

Estimated Year 1 funnel: 4,000 prospects identified by intelligence > 1,500 contacted > 400 campus visits or virtual engagements > 250 offers extended > 160 commitments (Varsity recruiting class). Development tier athletes add 60 enrollments for total athletic enrollment of approximately 220 in Year 1. Yield on offers (commitments divided by offers) is estimated at 60-70%, which is high relative to typical NAIA programs because of the scholarship, NIL, coaching, and intelligence advantages.

CAC for athletic recruits is low in dollar terms because the intelligence system replaces expensive scouting travel and recruiting services. The cost is embedded in coaching staff salaries and platform operations rather than per-student marketing spend.

## Enrollment Intelligence Architecture

The KaNeXT Enrollment Management Intelligence governs the full student lifecycle. Every channel, every conversion rate, every retention decision, and every forecast operates through governed components rather than through traditional enrollment marketing assumptions.

**Pipeline KR (PLKR).** Forecast accuracy, demographic health, market share, and pipeline diversity. PLKR governs enrollment projections across all four channels. Forecasts are never inflated above demographic pipeline plus fifteen percent. Every projection in this document is bounded by that governance rule, which is why the Year 5 targets sit below what aggressive but ungoverned projections would claim.

**Retention KR (RKR).** Primary retention rate, trend, cohort equity, and revenue impact of attrition. RKR governs retention outcomes at the cohort level and drives intervention decisions when early-warning signals appear.

**Persistence KR (PKR).** Completion and graduation rates, pace and momentum through the curriculum, progression pipeline integrity, and completion equity across populations. PKR governs the mid-cycle and end-cycle of the student lifecycle.

**Recovery KR (RCKR).** Early alert effectiveness, stop-out recovery, academic recovery programs, and root cause analysis. RCKR governs the recovery tier of the enrollment system when retention or persistence signals break.

Traditional enrollment projections are marketing estimates. KaNeXT projections are governed outputs from an intelligence system that tracks pipeline forecasting, retention cohort analysis, persistence risk flags, and recovery effectiveness in real time. The system identifies leaking bucket patterns, cliff-ahead demographic trajectories, aid-dependent retention risks, gateway gatekeeper courses, invisible departure patterns, and retention theater before they compound into enrollment collapse. The partner institution operates with this intelligence architecture deployed from Day 1. No peer institution operates with comparable enrollment governance.

## Retention As A Growth Component

Retention is not a footnote. Retention is a growth channel governed by the RKR component of the Enrollment Intelligence architecture.

**Baseline.** the partner institution's current retention rate is approximately 79% per the most recent institutional reporting. This is already above the national average for small Christian colleges, reflecting strong existing institutional identity and mission alignment.

**Target trajectory.**

| Year | Target Retention Rate |
|---|---|
| Year 1 | 80% - 82% |
| Year 2 | 82% - 84% |
| Year 3 | 84% - 86% |
| Year 4 | 85% - 87% |
| Year 5 | 86% - 88% |

The trajectory reflects realistic compounding improvement driven by the intelligence architecture, expanded student services, Dipson 24/7 advising, cluster housing community effects, athletic engagement for athletes and non-athletes alike, and the institutional quality investment described in the revenue model.

**Why retention matters for the revenue math.** A one-percentage-point retention improvement on a 10,000-student cohort produces 100 additional students retained per year. Compounded across the five-year horizon, retention improvement from 79% to 86% represents approximately 2,000-3,000 additional cumulative retained students versus the baseline retention rate. That is meaningful enrollment that does not require new acquisition spending. Retention at scale is the cheapest enrollment growth available, and the RKR intelligence system targets exactly this outcome.

The institutional quality investment described in the revenue model (faculty compensation above Christian university market, academic program development, student services excellence, campus infrastructure) is directly what earns retention. Students who experience premium institutional quality return at higher rates. The retention target trajectory is grounded in the quality investment, not aspirational.

## Key Assumptions and Sensitivities

**Channel certainty varies significantly.** Athletic recruiting is the channel closest to direct institutional control because it is driven by scholarship offers, intelligence-backed targeting, and the multi-level team structure. Brand-driven on-campus enrollment and online enrollment are more modeled and market-dependent, relying on media visibility, marketing effectiveness, and competitive positioning. International and diaspora enrollment is organic and relationship-driven but its pace is the hardest to predict with precision.

**Enrollment pace is the single most important assumption in the institutional revenue model.** If enrollment grows slower than projected, revenue declines faster than costs can be reduced. The intelligence architecture (PLKR, RKR, PKR, RCKR) is specifically designed to detect pace deterioration early and trigger intervention before small variance compounds into large shortfalls.

**Online enrollment is the most assumption-sensitive channel.** The Year 5 projection of approximately 12,600 online students is achievable given GCU and Liberty precedent but requires successful online program launches (accreditation and faculty approval), effective digital marketing at scale, competitive tuition pricing, and sustained brand momentum from athletic visibility and platform distribution.

**Athletic enrollment is the most predictable channel** because it is directly controlled through the intelligence system and scholarship offers. If the athletic system deploys as described, 220-900 athletes across Year 1 to Year 5 is realistic based on scholarship capacity and recruiting infrastructure.

**Retention compounds.** A 1% annual retention improvement across a growing enrollment base compounds significantly over five years. The retention target trajectory (79% baseline to 86-88% by Year 5) is grounded in the RKR intelligence architecture and the institutional quality investment that supports it. Retention below target reduces cumulative enrollment. Retention above target compounds enrollment favorably.

**CAC assumptions are modeled, not validated.** Actual CAC will depend on market conditions, competitive dynamics, and the effectiveness of the enrollment operation. CAC is expected to decline over time as organic channels grow through platform distribution, but the rate of decline is uncertain.
