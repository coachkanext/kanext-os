# COMPETITION SYSTEM SET

## Purpose
The Competition System Set defines the valid routine style selections available to coaches during Coach Context Setup. These describe the team's strategic philosophy for competition.

## Routine Styles (5)

1. **Difficulty-First** - Maximize difficulty values across all categories. Accept higher deduction risk in exchange for higher ceiling score. Teams with elite skill depth that can absorb occasional bobbles.
2. **Execution-First** - Prioritize clean, zero-deduction routines. Conservative difficulty choices. Win by hitting clean when others fall. Teams with consistent but not elite-level skills.
3. **Balanced** - Moderate difficulty with strong execution emphasis. Strategic difficulty upgrades only where the team has demonstrated mastery. Most common approach at competitive levels.
4. **Showmanship-First** - Maximize performance score and crowd engagement. Creative choreography, unique transitions, high-energy dance. Difficulty is secondary to presentation. Common in Game Day and some traditional formats.
5. **STUNT Match** - Head-to-head format. Quarter-by-quarter skill execution optimization. Different from routine-based styles because each quarter tests specific skill categories independently.

## Governance
Style names must exactly match across all downstream docs. No aliases permitted.

# TRAIT LIBRARY

## Trait Clusters - Canonical 5

### 1. Stunt Cluster (SKR)
Covers all partner stunt, group stunt, and pyramid skills.

### 2. Tumbling Cluster (TKR)
Covers all individual tumbling skills, standing and running.

### 3. Jump/Dance Cluster (JKR)
Covers jumps, dance technique, performance quality, and crowd engagement.

### 4. Athletic Cluster (AKR)
Covers physical tools: strength, flexibility, body control, spatial awareness, endurance.

### 5. Performance IQ Cluster (IQKR)
Covers mental game: routine execution under pressure, recovery, synchronization, leadership.

## Stunt Cluster - Traits (10)

### 1) Flyer Stability
The ability to hold body positions at extension without wobbling, shifting, or requiring base correction.
- Inputs: Video assessment of extension hold time, wobble frequency, body line maintenance
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Rock-solid at full extension in all positions. Zero visible correction needed. Holds 3+ seconds clean.
  - 80: Stable in most positions. Minor corrections in advanced positions (scorpion, needle). Holds 2+ seconds.
  - 70: Stable in standard positions (lib, heel stretch). Some wobble in advanced positions.
  - 60: Occasional wobble in standard positions. Needs base assistance to stabilize.
  - Below 60: Frequent instability. Not reliable at extension.

### 2) Base Strength/Technique
The ability to lift, support, and control stunts with proper hand placement, timing, and power.
- Inputs: Stunt level executed, weight supported, one-arm capability, consistency across repetitions
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: One-arm extended stunts with control. Can base any flyer at team weight range. Zero grip breaks.
  - 80: Two-arm extended stunts with confidence. Consistent toss power. Rare grip issues.
  - 70: Prep-level stunts clean. Extension stunts with occasional technique breaks.
  - 60: Prep-level stunts with effort. Extension inconsistent.
  - Below 60: Below prep-level reliability.

### 3) Toss Execution
Height, control, and timing of basket tosses and other toss-based skills.
- Inputs: Toss height achieved, flyer rotation time available, catch consistency, timing with partner group
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Elite toss height (12+ feet visible air). Flyer has time for kick double or equivalent. 95%+ catch rate.
  - 80: Strong toss height (10-12 feet). Flyer has time for kick full. 90%+ catch rate.
  - 70: Adequate toss height (8-10 feet). Flyer can execute toe touch or single rotation. 85%+ catch rate.
  - 60: Low toss height. Limited flyer skills possible. Catch consistency below 85%.
  - Below 60: Toss not competition-ready.

### 4) Catch Consistency
Reliability of catching flyers on dismounts and tosses. Safety-critical trait.
- Inputs: Drop rate, catch position quality, recovery on off-axis catches
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Zero drops in competition setting. Clean catch position every time. Adjusts seamlessly to off-axis.
  - 80: Zero drops. Occasional position adjustment needed. Handles most off-axis catches.
  - 70: Rare drops (1 per season or less). Adequate catch position. Some off-axis difficulty.
  - 60: Occasional drops (2-3 per season). Catch position inconsistent.
  - Below 60: Drop frequency is a safety concern.

### 5) Pyramid Stability
Contribution to multi-person pyramid structures. Varies by role (top, middle, base).
- Inputs: Pyramid completion rate, position hold quality, transition execution within pyramid
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Elite pyramid contributor. Holds through complex transitions. Enables highest-difficulty pyramid skills.
  - 80: Strong pyramid contributor. Reliable in standard pyramids. Handles most transitions.
  - 70: Adequate pyramid contributor. Performs in standard pyramids. Some difficulty with advanced transitions.
  - 60: Can participate but limits pyramid difficulty options.
  - Below 60: Pyramid participation is a liability.

### 6) Partner/Group Synchronization
Timing and coordination with stunt partners and group members.
- Inputs: Visual sync assessment, count timing accuracy, transition smoothness
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Perfect sync. Movements indistinguishable from partners in timing and amplitude. Elite visual unity.
  - 80: Strong sync. Minor timing variations visible only on slow-motion review.
  - 70: Adequate sync. Occasional visible timing differences in transitions.
  - 60: Sync issues visible to judges. Timing breaks in transitions.
  - Below 60: Sync problems affect team scoring.

### 7) Transition Execution
Ability to perform skill-to-skill transitions (tic-tocks, switch-ups, reloads) smoothly.
- Inputs: Transition type difficulty, completion rate, speed, visual cleanliness
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Elite transitions. Tic-tock to lib, switch-ups, full-up variations. Clean and fast.
  - 80: Strong transitions. Standard tic-tocks and switch-ups with control.
  - 70: Basic transitions executed. Speed and cleanliness are adequate.
  - 60: Transitions are slow or show visible effort/break.
  - Below 60: Transitions not competition-ready.

### 8) Dismount Execution
Quality and difficulty of stunt dismounts (cradles, twist cradles, kick variations).
- Inputs: Dismount difficulty, landing/catch quality, timing
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Kick double or equivalent dismount. Perfect catch position. Elite timing.
  - 80: Twist cradle or kick full. Clean catch. Consistent.
  - 70: Standard cradle with good pop. Adequate catch position.
  - 60: Basic cradle. Low pop or inconsistent catch.
  - Below 60: Dismount is a deduction risk.

### 9) One-Arm Capability (Base-specific)
Ability to perform single-base extended stunts using one arm.
- Inputs: One-arm stunt level achieved, hold time, stability
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: One-arm extended liberty with control. Can walk, turn, and transition one-arm.
  - 80: One-arm extended stunt with hold. Limited movement while one-arm.
  - 70: One-arm prep-level stunt. Working toward extension.
  - 60: One-arm at waist level only.
  - Below 60: No one-arm capability.

### 10) Body Position Mastery (Flyer-specific)
Range and quality of body positions held in stunts and pyramids.
- Inputs: Positions demonstrated (lib, heel stretch, arabesque, scale, scorpion, needle, bow and arrow), hold quality
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: All advanced positions (needle, bow and arrow) with clean lines and full extension. Flexibility is elite.
  - 80: Advanced positions (scorpion, scale) with good lines. Most positions in inventory.
  - 70: Standard positions (lib, heel stretch, arabesque) with adequate lines.
  - 60: Limited positions. Lib and basic positions only.
  - Below 60: Cannot reliably hold standard positions.

## Tumbling Cluster - Traits (8)

### 1) Standing Tumbling Difficulty
Highest standing tumbling skill demonstrated with competition-ready execution.
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Standing double full or higher. Elite-level standing tumbling.
  - 80: Standing full (layout with full twist from standing). Clean landing.
  - 70: Standing layout or standing back tuck with good form.
  - 60: Standing back handspring series. No standing aerial/tuck.
  - Below 60: Below standing back handspring.

### 2) Running Tumbling Difficulty
Highest running tumbling pass demonstrated with competition-ready execution.
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Running double layout, double full, or whip-to-double. Elite power tumbling.
  - 80: Running full (layout full twist) or layout-to-full. Clean and powerful.
  - 70: Running layout. Clean form. Good rebound.
  - 60: Running back tuck. Round-off back handspring series.
  - Below 60: Below running back tuck.

### 3) Connected Tumbling
Ability to link tumbling skills in combination passes.
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Elite connections (whip-to-double, full-to-layout, punch front-to-full). Multiple skills linked.
  - 80: Strong connections (round-off back handspring-to-full, standing series-to-tuck).
  - 70: Standard connections (round-off back handspring-to-layout or tuck).
  - 60: Basic connections (back handspring series). No skill-to-skill connections.
  - Below 60: No connected tumbling.

### 4) Tumbling Execution
Form, technique, and landing quality across all tumbling skills.
- Inputs: Leg separation, arm position, body line, landing stick rate, step/hop frequency
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Textbook form. Clean lines. Sticks landings consistently. Zero visible technique breaks.
  - 80: Strong form. Minor deductions possible. Lands clean most of the time.
  - 70: Adequate form. Some visible technique issues (bent legs, arm position). Occasional step on landing.
  - 60: Noticeable technique breaks. Frequent landing issues. Deduction-prone.
  - Below 60: Technique issues are safety concerns.

### 5) Tumbling Power
Height, distance, and speed of tumbling passes. The raw athletic output.
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Exceptional height and distance. Tumbling passes cover the floor with power to spare.
  - 80: Strong power. Good height and speed. Completes skills with room.
  - 70: Adequate power. Gets through skills but not overpowering.
  - 60: Limited power. Skills feel effortful. Height/distance marginal.
  - Below 60: Insufficient power for skills attempted.

### 6) Jump-to-Tumbling
Ability to connect jumps directly into tumbling passes (toe touch-back tuck, etc.).
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Elite jump-to-tumbling (toe touch-full, pike-to-back tuck). Clean connection with no pause.
  - 80: Strong jump-to-tumbling (toe touch-back tuck or handspring). Minimal pause.
  - 70: Adequate connection. Jump-to-back handspring or similar. Slight hesitation.
  - 60: Basic jump-to-tumbling. Visible break between jump and skill.
  - Below 60: Cannot connect jumps to tumbling.

### 7) Standing Series
Length and difficulty of standing back handspring series and connected standing skills.
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: 4+ standing back handsprings with speed and control, connected to standing tuck or better.
  - 80: 3+ standing back handsprings connected to a skill (tuck, layout).
  - 70: 2-3 standing back handsprings. Consistent form.
  - 60: 1-2 standing back handsprings. Adequate form.
  - Below 60: No standing back handspring.

### 8) Specialty Tumbling
Unique or less common tumbling skills that add routine value (front tumbling, aerials, specialty passes).
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Multiple specialty skills (standing Arabian, front full, X-out, specialty whips). Routine differentiators.
  - 80: 1-2 specialty skills demonstrated. Adds unique value to routine.
  - 70: Front walkover or front handspring variations. Basic specialty.
  - 60: No specialty tumbling. Standard skills only.
  - Below 60: N/A (lack of specialty is not a deficiency, just a ceiling limiter).

## Jump/Dance Cluster - Traits (8)

### 1) Jump Height
Vertical elevation on cheerleading jumps (toe touch, pike, hurdler, etc.).
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Hips above shoulders on all jumps. Elite vertical. Visible "hang time."
  - 80: Hips at or near shoulder level. Strong vertical. Clean peak.
  - 70: Adequate height. Hips approach waist level. Visible effort but clean.
  - 60: Below average height. Jumps are flat. Limited elevation.
  - Below 60: Jump height is a deduction liability.

### 2) Jump Technique
Form and execution of jumps - toe point, chest position, arm placement, leg position.
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Perfect form. Pointed toes, T-arms, chest up, hyperextended legs. Textbook.
  - 80: Strong form. Minor imperfections. Consistent technique across jump types.
  - 70: Adequate form. Some visible issues (flexed feet, low chest). Consistent enough for competition.
  - 60: Technique issues visible to judges. Multiple form breaks.
  - Below 60: Technique is a consistent deduction source.

### 3) Jump Combinations
Ability to perform multiple jumps in sequence (toe touch-pike, toe touch-right hurdler-left hurdler, etc.).
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: 3+ jump combination with consistent height and technique throughout. No drop-off.
  - 80: 2-jump combination with strong execution. Height maintained on second jump.
  - 70: 2-jump combination with some height loss on second jump. Adequate.
  - 60: Single jumps only. Cannot maintain height in combinations.
  - Below 60: N/A

### 4) Dance Sharpness
Precision and sharpness of movements during choreographed dance sections.
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Every movement is crisp, precise, and intentional. Zero sloppiness. Elite precision.
  - 80: Strong sharpness. Consistent throughout routine. Minor soft moments in complex choreography.
  - 70: Adequate sharpness. Some movements lack full extension or snap.
  - 60: Noticeable softness. Movements don't fully complete. Judge-visible.
  - Below 60: Dance sections are a scoring liability.

### 5) Dance Musicality
Ability to move on beat, interpret music, and match movement quality to musical dynamics.
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Perfect musicality. Anticipates beats. Movement quality matches musical dynamics perfectly.
  - 80: Strong musicality. On beat consistently. Responds to dynamics.
  - 70: Adequate musicality. On beat most of the time. Occasionally behind or ahead.
  - 60: Inconsistent musicality. Noticeable timing issues.
  - Below 60: Frequently off beat.

### 6) Performance Quality
Facial expression, energy projection, crowd connection, and showmanship during performance.
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Captivating performer. Commands attention. Energy never drops. Natural stage presence.
  - 80: Strong performer. Consistent energy. Engages audience. Confident facial expressions.
  - 70: Adequate performance. Mostly engaged. Some moments of dropped energy or blank face.
  - 60: Performance quality is inconsistent. Appears nervous or disconnected at times.
  - Below 60: Performance quality drags the team score.

### 7) Crowd Engagement (Game Day/Sideline specific)
Ability to lead a crowd, project voice, and create energy in a sideline/game day context.
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Natural crowd leader. Loud, clear voice. Crowd follows every cheer. Energy is infectious.
  - 80: Strong crowd engagement. Good voice projection. Crowd responds.
  - 70: Adequate. Can lead cheers but doesn't dominate the crowd's attention.
  - 60: Limited crowd engagement. Voice doesn't carry or crowd doesn't respond.
  - Below 60: Not effective in crowd-leading role.

### 8) Choreography Retention
Speed and accuracy of learning and retaining choreography. Important for team synchronization.
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Learns choreography in 1-2 run-throughs. Retains perfectly. Can teach others.
  - 80: Learns quickly. Solid retention. Minor details may need reinforcement.
  - 70: Average learning speed. Needs multiple repetitions. Retains adequately once learned.
  - 60: Slow learner. Needs significant repetition. Sometimes forgets sequences.
  - Below 60: Choreography retention is a team liability.

## Athletic Cluster - Traits (8)

### 1) Functional Strength
Relevant strength for cheer-specific tasks. NOT just gym strength - functional application.
- For Bases: Overhead press capacity, squat strength, grip strength
- For Flyers: Core strength, isometric hold capacity, pull-up/body weight ratio
- For Back Spots: Catch strength, overhead reach strength, lateral stability
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Elite functional strength for role. Exceeds role demands. Can compensate for partner weakness.
  - 80: Strong for role. Meets all demands. Consistent across practice and competition.
  - 70: Adequate for role. Meets most demands. May fatigue in back-to-back skills.
  - 60: Meets minimum role demands. Strength is a limiting factor in skill progression.
  - Below 60: Below role strength requirements. Safety concern.

### 2) Flexibility
Range of motion relevant to cheer skills. Especially critical for flyers.
- Inputs: Split quality (right, left, center), shoulder flexibility, back flexibility, hip flexibility
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Oversplits. Elite back flexibility (chest-to-floor bridge). Full shoulder rotation. Needle/bow-arrow capable.
  - 80: Flat splits all three ways. Good back flexibility. Scorpion capable.
  - 70: Flat splits (right/left). Adequate back flexibility. Heel stretch capable.
  - 60: Approaching flat splits. Limited back flexibility. Basic positions only.
  - Below 60: Flexibility limits role options.

### 3) Body Control
Ability to control body position in the air, during skills, and in held positions. Core engagement.
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Elite body awareness. Tight body position in all situations. Air awareness for flips and tosses.
  - 80: Strong body control. Consistent tightness. Good air awareness.
  - 70: Adequate control. Occasional looseness in complex skills.
  - 60: Noticeable control issues. Body position breaks in difficult skills.
  - Below 60: Body control is a safety and scoring issue.

### 4) Spatial Awareness
Understanding of where the body is in space relative to the ground, partners, and other athletes.
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Elite spatial awareness. Perfect landing positioning. Adjusts mid-skill. Never collides with others.
  - 80: Strong awareness. Consistent positioning. Rare spatial errors.
  - 70: Adequate. Occasional misjudgments in complex formations.
  - 60: Spatial errors are noticeable. Requires coaching reminders for spacing.
  - Below 60: Spatial awareness is a safety concern.

### 5) Endurance
Ability to maintain skill quality and energy throughout an entire routine (typically 2:30) or STUNT match (4 quarters).
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: No drop-off from start to finish. Same energy, same execution quality. Can perform back-to-back routines.
  - 80: Minimal drop-off. Last 30 seconds may show slight fatigue. Recovery between attempts is fast.
  - 70: Some noticeable fatigue in final section. Tumbling or jump height decreases.
  - 60: Clear fatigue impact. Skills degrade in second half of routine.
  - Below 60: Endurance limits competitive viability.

### 6) Injury Resilience
History of staying healthy, ability to perform through minor discomfort, recovery speed.
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: No significant injury history. Durable. Practices and competes at full capacity consistently.
  - 80: Minor injury history. Recovers quickly. Rarely misses competitions.
  - 70: Some injury history. Misses occasional practice. Has competed through minor issues.
  - 60: Recurring injury issues. Misses multiple competitions or practices per season.
  - Below 60: Chronic injury concerns that affect availability and performance.

### 7) Speed/Quickness
Relevant for tumbling acceleration, transition speed, and formation changes.
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Elite speed. Tumbling passes are fast. Transitions are instant. Formation changes are sharp.
  - 80: Strong speed. Quick tumbling. Fast transitions.
  - 70: Adequate speed. Keeps pace with team. Not the fastest.
  - 60: Noticeably slower. Timing issues in fast-paced sections.
  - Below 60: Speed limits team choreography options.

### 8) Weight Management / Body Composition
Relevant for flyer/base pairings and safety. Evaluated relative to role, not to aesthetic standards. This is a functional assessment only.
- For Flyers: Body weight relative to base capability. Lighter flyers are mechanically easier to stunt with, but this is a function of the partnership, not an absolute standard.
- For Bases: Strength-to-body-weight ratio. Bigger bases can handle more weight but need proportional technique.
- Bands: NOT scored numerically. This trait is flagged only when a mismatch creates a SAFETY concern (e.g., base cannot safely support flyer weight at extension). It is never used for aesthetic judgment.
- SAFETY FLAG ONLY. No band scoring. No KR impact unless safety threshold is crossed.

## Performance IQ Cluster - Traits (7)

### 1) Routine Execution Under Pressure
Ability to perform at the same level (or better) in competition as in practice.
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Competition enhances performance. Hits hardest skills under pressure. Zero nerves visible.
  - 80: Competition-ready. Performs at practice level. Occasional minor competition nerves don't affect execution.
  - 70: Adequate under pressure. Some skills show slight degradation in competition.
  - 60: Noticeable performance drop in competition. Nerves affect execution.
  - Below 60: Competition anxiety significantly impacts performance.

### 2) Recovery from Mistakes
Ability to continue performing at a high level after a bobble, fall, or mistake mid-routine.
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Instant recovery. Mistake is invisible by the next 8-count. Maintains energy and expression.
  - 80: Quick recovery. Returns to full performance within a few counts. No cascading errors.
  - 70: Recovers but it takes a section. Some visible impact on next skill.
  - 60: Recovery is slow. Mistake cascades into next skill or visibly affects energy.
  - Below 60: Mistakes derail remaining performance.

### 3) Energy Management
Ability to pace energy across a routine to peak at the right moments.
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Perfect energy curve. Peaks at high-impact moments. Never wastes energy. Crescendo into finale.
  - 80: Strong energy management. Consistent throughout. Hits most peaks.
  - 70: Adequate. Some energy dips in transition sections.
  - 60: Energy is inconsistent. Starts strong, fades. Or starts slow, recovers.
  - Below 60: Energy management is not evident.

### 4) Synchronization Awareness
Ability to stay in sync with teammates through peripheral vision, musical cues, and count awareness.
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Naturally in sync. Adjusts to teammates without looking. Peripheral awareness is elite. Team metronome.
  - 80: Strong sync. Uses musical cues and peripheral vision effectively.
  - 70: Adequate sync. Occasionally a half-count off. Relies on direct visual cues.
  - 60: Sync issues are noticeable. Frequently a count behind or ahead.
  - Below 60: Sync is a consistent deduction source.

### 5) Showmanship
The "it factor" - natural performance presence that elevates the team's overall presentation score.
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Star performer. Judges notice this athlete. Elevates every section they're in. Natural entertainer.
  - 80: Strong showmanship. Adds to team presentation. Consistent performance face.
  - 70: Adequate showmanship. Doesn't detract. Doesn't stand out.
  - 60: Underwhelming presentation. Flat expression. Limited crowd connection.
  - Below 60: Showmanship is absent.

### 6) Leadership
Vocal and behavioral leadership within the team. Especially critical for back spots (who call counts) and team captains.
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Team leader. Sets the standard. Calls counts with authority. Teammates respond to their energy.
  - 80: Strong leader. Reliable voice. Leads by example.
  - 70: Adequate. Follows team structure. Not a primary leader but contributes.
  - 60: Passive. Does not lead. Follows only.
  - Below 60: N/A (not a deficiency, but limits back spot and captain viability).

### 7) Coachability
Ability to receive feedback and implement corrections quickly. Critical for development trajectory.
- Bands (v0):
  - 90: Implements corrections immediately. Seeks feedback. Self-corrects before coach intervenes.
  - 80: Receptive to feedback. Implements within a few repetitions.
  - 70: Accepts feedback. Takes time to implement. Needs repeated reminders.
  - 60: Resistant to feedback or slow to implement. Repeats same errors.
  - Below 60: Uncoachable behavior or inability to process corrections.

# ARCHETYPE LIBRARY

## Purpose
Archetypes describe an athlete's functional identity based on their trait profile. Each athlete receives a primary archetype and may qualify for a secondary.

## Archetypes (15)

### 1. Elite Flyer
- Profile: SKR 85+, AKR(flexibility) 85+, AKR(body control) 85+, low weight relative to bases
- Description: Top-tier flyer who enables the highest stunt difficulty. Stable, flexible, and tight. The athlete the routine is built around in the air.
- System fit: Essential for Difficulty-First teams. High value in any format.

### 2. Power Flyer
- Profile: SKR 80+, AKR(strength) 80+, TKR 75+, moderate flexibility
- Description: Flyer with strong tumbling and athletic tools. May not have elite flexibility but compensates with body control and power. Can fly AND tumble.
- System fit: Valuable for balanced teams. Versatility increases routine options.

### 3. Precision Flyer
- Profile: SKR 80+, IQKR(sync) 85+, JKR(dance) 80+
- Description: Flyer who excels at clean execution and synchronization rather than extreme difficulty. Zero-deduction performer.
- System fit: Ideal for Execution-First teams.

### 4. Power Base
- Profile: SKR(base strength) 85+, AKR(functional strength) 85+, AKR(endurance) 75+
- Description: Elite base who can handle any flyer at any difficulty level. One-arm capable. Toss machine. The foundation of stunt difficulty.
- System fit: Essential for Difficulty-First teams.

### 5. Technical Base
- Profile: SKR(base strength) 75+, SKR(sync) 85+, IQKR 80+
- Description: Base whose strength is precision and timing rather than raw power. Excellent hand placement, smooth transitions, reliable catches.
- System fit: Ideal for Execution-First and Balanced teams.

### 6. Anchor Base
- Profile: SKR(base strength) 80+, SKR(catch) 90+, IQKR(leadership) 80+
- Description: The base you trust in the biggest moment. Not always the strongest but never drops. Safety anchor for the stunt group.
- System fit: High value in any format. Back spot potential.

### 7. Elite Back Spot
- Profile: SKR(catch) 90+, AKR(strength) 80+, IQKR(leadership) 85+
- Description: Elite safety position. Never misses a catch. Calls counts with authority. Controls the tempo of every stunt. Team glue.
- System fit: Critical for all teams. Limited supply of elite back spots.

### 8. Tumbling Specialist
- Profile: TKR 85+, standing and running, connected tumbling
- Description: Elite tumbler whose primary value is tumbling passes. May or may not contribute to stunts. Tumbling is the weapon.
- System fit: High value for Difficulty-First teams in tumbling-scored sections.

### 9. Power Tumbler
- Profile: TKR 80+, AKR(speed, strength) 80+
- Description: Raw tumbling power - high, fast, powerful passes. May lack some precision but the power is elite.
- System fit: Adds difficulty points. Valuable in STUNT Q2 (jumps/tumbling).

### 10. All-Around Elite
- Profile: SKR 80+, TKR 80+, JKR 75+, AKR 80+, IQKR 80+
- Description: Can play any role at a high level. The Swiss Army knife. Can fly, can base, can tumble, can dance. Most valuable athlete type in cheer.
- System fit: Maximizes roster flexibility. Covers for injuries. Enables more complex routine construction.

### 11. Performance Anchor
- Profile: JKR 85+, IQKR(showmanship) 85+, IQKR(sync) 80+
- Description: The performer whose energy and presence elevate the entire team's presentation score. Center-front in formations. The one judges watch.
- System fit: Essential for Showmanship-First teams. High value in Game Day format.

### 12. Game Day Specialist
- Profile: JKR(crowd engagement) 85+, IQKR(showmanship) 80+, JKR(dance) 75+
- Description: Excels specifically in sideline and game day formats. Crowd leading, band dance, fight song, time-out performances.
- System fit: High value for programs with Game Day competition format.

### 13. Pyramid Specialist
- Profile: SKR(pyramid stability) 85+, AKR(spatial awareness) 85+, AKR(body control) 80+
- Description: Excels specifically in pyramid construction. May be a middle-layer athlete who enables complex pyramid transitions. Understands multi-person timing.
- System fit: Enables higher pyramid difficulty. Valuable in Q3 of STUNT.

### 14. Developmental Athlete
- Profile: No component KR above 75 but AKR shows physical tools and IQKR(coachability) is 75+
- Description: Not yet competition-ready at the current level but has the raw tools and coachability to develop. Investment athlete.
- System fit: Roster depth. Future value. Development plan required.

### 15. Utility Contributor
- Profile: Multiple component KRs in the 70-79 range. No standout skill but no liability.
- Description: Reliable contributor who won't win you a competition but won't lose it either. Fills gaps. Covers absences. Steady.
- System fit: Roster depth. Practice quality. Backup for any role.

# ROUTINE SYSTEM DEMAND PROFILES

## Purpose
Each Routine Style creates different demands for athlete archetypes. Demand Tiers (A/B/C/No-match) determine how much an athlete's archetype is valued in each system.

### Difficulty-First Demands
| Tier | Archetypes |
|------|-----------|

# OVERRIDES

Overrides are hard rules that modify KR output regardless of what the math produces.

## Safety Override
If any safety flag is RED (Block), the athlete's KR for that component is capped at the difficulty level they have demonstrated safely. No projection, no ceiling raise. Safety overrides math.

## Role Mismatch Override
If an athlete is evaluated in a role they have never performed in competition, all component KRs except AKR and IQKR receive a -5 uncertainty penalty and confidence is capped at 60%. This prevents projecting a tumbler as a flyer (or vice versa) without evidence.

## Freshness Override
If an athlete has not competed in 12+ months, a -3 freshness penalty applies to all component KRs and confidence is capped at 70% until new competition data is available.

## Injury Override
If an athlete has a declared unresolved injury affecting a specific skill category, that component KR is flagged UNSCORED until medical clearance is documented. The overall KR is computed from remaining components with adjusted weights and reduced confidence.

# SYSTEM RISKS

System Risks flag structural concerns that may not show up in component KRs but affect team value.

| Risk | Trigger | Impact |
|------|---------|--------|
| Role Lock | Athlete can only perform one role | Limits roster flexibility. Single-role athletes reduce depth. |
| Partner Dependency | Athlete's stunt performance varies significantly by partner | KR may not transfer to new partner group. Flag when evaluating for different team. |
| Peak Difficulty Plateau | Athlete has not gained a new skill level in 2+ seasons | Development ceiling concern. May be at maximum ability. |
| Competition Anxiety | Documented performance drop in high-stakes competitions | IQKR penalty in competition settings. Development plan should address. |
| Injury Recurrence | Same injury type 2+ times | Availability risk. Affects long-term value and role assignment. |
| Weight Trajectory | Weight trend creates emerging safety concern for role | Must be handled with extreme sensitivity. Flag only when functional safety threshold is approached. |

# KLVN (Competition Level Normalization)

## Purpose
KLVN normalizes skill difficulty and competition intensity across different competitive levels so that KR remains a universal measure. A toe touch at the recreational level is not the same as a toe touch at Level 6 All-Star. KLVN adjusts the inputs (skill difficulty context), not the KR output.

## Lambda Values

See **College_Cheer_KLVN_Lambdas_v1.md** for the full lambda table.

## How KLVN Works in Cheer
1. Identify the athlete's competitive level
2. Look up the lambda value for that level
3. During trait scoring, adjust the band thresholds by lambda
   - Example: If lambda = 0.85 and a "90 band" tumbling skill requires a standing full at the reference level, the 90 band at the 0.85 level requires approximately standing layout (one difficulty tier lower)
4. Score the athlete against the adjusted bands
5. Record the KR - this is the UNIVERSAL KR
6. To interpret the KR at different levels, read it against different legend files (Level Tier Map)

## Key Rule
KR is NEVER multiplied by lambda. Lambda adjusts scoring inputs. KR is one number. The Level Tier Map shows what that one number means at different levels.
