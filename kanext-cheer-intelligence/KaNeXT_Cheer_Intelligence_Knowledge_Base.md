## The Sport: Competitive Cheer and STUNT

### Overview
Competitive cheerleading is an athletic discipline that combines stunting (partner and group lifts/tosses), tumbling (gymnastics-derived flipping and twisting skills), jumping, dance, and performance. It is performed by teams ranging from 5 to 36+ athletes depending on the format and division.

There are two primary competitive formats at the college level:
1. **STUNT** - A head-to-head team sport recognized as an NCAA Emerging Sport for Women. Four quarters, each testing different skill categories. Scored by judges using a standardized rubric. More measurable and sport-like than traditional cheer.
2. **Traditional Competition Cheer** - Teams perform a 2:30 routine judged by a panel. Scored across categories (stunts, tumbling, pyramids, jumps, dance, overall impression). Events include NCA College Nationals, UCA College Nationals, and conference championships.

At the All-Star level, teams are organized by age, size, and skill level (Level 1 through Level 6). The pinnacle is the Cheerleading Worlds, organized by the USASF/IASF.

### Why Cheer Is Different From Ball Sports
- **Judged, not scored.** There is no ball going through a hoop or into a goal. Performance is evaluated by human judges using rubrics. This introduces inherent subjectivity.
- **Team execution, not individual stats.** The team performs a routine as a unit. There are no individual box scores. Individual contribution must be inferred from role assignment and skill difficulty.
- **Skills-based, not play-based.** Athletes have a defined inventory of skills they can perform. Evaluation is based on this inventory plus execution quality, not on game-by-game statistical variation.
- **Safety is a primary concern.** Athletes are lifted, thrown, and flipped. Falls from height are a real risk. The evaluation system must treat safety as non-negotiable.
- **Roles are specialized.** A flyer and a base are as different as a quarterback and an offensive lineman. Evaluation must be role-specific.

### STUNT Format Deep Dive
STUNT was founded to provide a more sport-like framework for competitive cheer. Key features:
- **Head-to-head competition.** Two teams compete directly, side by side, performing the same skill category simultaneously.
- **Four quarters:** Q1 (Partner Stunts), Q2 (Jumps/Tumbling), Q3 (Pyramids/Tosses), Q4 (Team Performance).
- **Objective scoring.** Each team performs skills; judges score execution, difficulty, and synchronization. Higher score wins the quarter. Win 3 of 4 quarters to win the match.
- **Growing rapidly.** STUNT has 100+ varsity and club programs and is on track for NCAA championship status. It was granted Emerging Sport for Women status to encourage growth.
- **Scholarship eligible.** NCAA schools can offer scholarships for STUNT, though availability varies by institution and conference.

### All-Star System
The All-Star system is the primary pathway for youth and developmental athletes:
- **Level 1-2:** Introductory. Basic stunts, handsprings, simple jumps. Ages typically 5-12.
- **Level 3:** Intermediate. Back tucks, extension stunts, jump combinations. Ages typically 10-15.
- **Level 4:** Advanced. Layouts, full-ups, pyramid transitions. Ages typically 12-18.
- **Level 5:** Elite pre-Worlds. Full twisting tumbling, elite stunts, complex pyramids. Ages typically 14-18.
- **Level 6 (International Open):** Unrestricted difficulty. The highest level. No age minimum but athletes are typically 16+. Worlds competition is the goal.

### Governing Bodies
- **USASF (U.S. All Star Federation):** Governs All-Star cheerleading in the US. Sets level rules and sanctions Worlds.
- **IASF (International All-Star Federation):** International governing body for All-Star cheer.
- **USA Cheer:** National governing body for cheerleading in the US. Oversees safety, coach certification, and STUNT.
- **NCAA:** STUNT is an NCAA Emerging Sport. Individual institutions govern their STUNT programs under NCAA rules.
- **NCA (National Cheerleaders Association):** Hosts NCA College Nationals and All-Star Nationals.
- **UCA (Universal Cheerleaders Association):** Hosts UCA College Nationals and International All-Star Championship.
- **NAIA:** Some NAIA institutions have competitive cheer programs.

## Competition Scoring Rubrics

### STUNT Scoring (Simplified)
Each quarter, judges evaluate:
- **Execution (40-50% of quarter score):** Were the skills performed cleanly? Deductions for bobbles, falls, technique breaks.
- **Difficulty (30-40% of quarter score):** How hard were the skills attempted? Higher difficulty = higher potential score.
- **Synchronization (10-20% of quarter score):** Were team members in sync? Visual unity. Timing precision.

The team with the higher score wins the quarter. Match is best 3 of 4 quarters.

### Traditional Competition Scoring (Simplified)
A 2:30 routine is scored across categories:
- Stunts (typically 20-25% of total)
- Tumbling (typically 15-20%)
- Pyramids (typically 15-20%)
- Jumps (typically 10-15%)
- Dance (typically 10-15%)
- Overall Impression / Performance (typically 10-15%)

Each category is scored on execution, difficulty, and technique. Deductions are taken for errors. Total score determines placement.

### All-Star Scoring
Similar to traditional competition scoring but with level-specific rules that restrict which skills are legal. A perfectly executed L4 routine cannot score as high as a perfectly executed L6 routine because the difficulty ceiling is lower.

## Safety Standards

### USA Cheer Safety Rules (Key Points)
- Spotters are required for all overhead stunts during practice
- Minimum number of bases required for each stunt type
- Height restrictions for pyramids (2.5 body lengths maximum for most levels)
- Surface requirements (mats, spring floor) for specific skills
- Coach certification required (AACCA safety certification or equivalent)
- Concussion protocols (return-to-activity clearance required)
- Progressive skill development required (cannot attempt advanced skills without demonstrated mastery of prerequisites)

### STUNT-Specific Safety
- Medical personnel required at all matches
- Warm-up time guaranteed before competition
- Skills that violate safety rules result in automatic loss of the point (not just a deduction)
- Head coaches must hold current USA Cheer safety certification

## Athlete Development Pathways

### Typical Elite Path
1. Recreational tumbling/gymnastics classes (ages 4-7)
2. All-Star Level 1-2 (ages 6-10)
3. All-Star Level 3-4 (ages 10-14)
4. All-Star Level 5 (ages 14-17)
5. All-Star Level 6 and/or college STUNT/competition (ages 16-22)
6. Post-college: All-Star coaching, college coaching, professional performance, or retirement

### Non-Traditional Paths
- **Gymnast converts:** Former gymnasts who transition to cheer (often as tumblers). Tumbling is elite but stunt/partner skills need development.
- **Dancer converts:** Former dancers who transition to cheer (often strong in performance/dance categories). Technical cheer skills need development.
- **Late starters:** Athletes who begin competitive cheer in high school. May have raw athleticism but need accelerated skill development.
- **Multi-sport athletes:** Athletes who do cheer alongside other sports. Schedule management and injury risk must be considered.

## College Recruiting Landscape

### NCAA STUNT Recruiting
- Programs are growing. New programs launch every year.
- Scholarship availability varies. Some programs are fully funded, others offer partial or no athletic scholarships.
- Many STUNT athletes come from strong All-Star backgrounds.
- Recruiting events include NCA, UCA, and regional showcases.
- Film/video is the primary evaluation tool for coaches who cannot attend live events.
- STUNT-specific recruiting is newer than traditional sports recruiting. The infrastructure is still developing.

### NCA/UCA Recruiting
- Traditional competition programs recruit through tryout processes.
- Many college teams recruit from local/regional All-Star programs.
- Game Day and sideline teams may have different tryout criteria than competition teams.
- Some programs recruit nationally; most recruit regionally.

## Key Metrics and Terminology

### Skills Terminology
- **Stunt:** Any skill where one or more athletes support another athlete above the ground.
- **Toss/Basket Toss:** Athletes throw a flyer into the air; she performs a skill at the peak, then is caught.
- **Pyramid:** Multiple stunt groups connected, with athletes at multiple height levels.
- **Tumbling:** Individual gymnastics-derived skills performed on the ground (flips, twists, handsprings).
- **Liberty (Lib):** A stunt position where the flyer stands on one foot at extension (overhead).
- **Full-Up:** The flyer performs a full twist while being lifted to the top of the stunt.
- **Tic-Tock:** A transition where the flyer moves from one foot to the other at extension.
- **Cradle:** A dismount where the flyer is caught by bases in a lying position.
- **Kick Full/Double:** A dismount where the flyer performs 1 or 2 full twists while being caught.
- **Braced Flip:** A pyramid skill where a flyer performs an inversion (flip) while connected to other athletes.
- **Hit (verb):** To complete a skill cleanly with no deductions. "They hit their routine."
- **Full-Out:** A routine performed at full speed and difficulty with no stops. The competition run.
- **Zero-Deduction:** A routine with no visible errors. The gold standard.

### Scoring Terminology
- **DV (Difficulty Value):** The point value assigned to a skill based on its difficulty.
- **Execution Score:** Points awarded for the quality of performance, reduced by deductions.
- **Deduction:** Points subtracted for errors (bobbles, falls, technique breaks, timing issues).
- **Overall Impression (OI):** A category where judges score the routine's general quality, energy, and presentation.
- **Hit Rate:** The percentage of competitions where the team completes the routine without a major error.

## Program Tiers (Reference)

### Tier 1: Elite Programs
Programs with national championship contention history, fully resourced coaching staff, strong recruiting pipelines, and institutional support. Examples in STUNT: Baylor, Oregon, Davenport.

### Tier 2: National Competitive
Programs that consistently qualify for national events. Strong athletes, good coaching, competitive budgets. Multiple NCAA D1 and D2 STUNT programs fall here.

### Tier 3: Conference Competitive
Programs that compete well within their conference but are not yet national contenders. Growing programs, developing rosters, building recruiting pipelines.

### Tier 4: Developing
Newer programs or programs with limited resources. Building athlete bases. Focused on growth and improvement year-over-year.

### Tier 5: Emerging/New
Programs in their first 1-3 years. Establishing identity. Recruiting first full rosters. Learning the competitive landscape.
