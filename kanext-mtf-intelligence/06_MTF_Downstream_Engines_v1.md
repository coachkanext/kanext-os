# MEN'S TRACK AND FIELD - DOWNSTREAM ENGINES v1

---

## 0. Purpose

The Downstream Engines consume finalized athlete KRs and produce actionable intelligence for development, placement, and professional transition. These engines do NOT evaluate athletes. They do NOT modify KR. They read governed truth and produce recommendations only.

# PART 1: DEVELOPMENT INTELLIGENCE ENGINE

---

## 1. Purpose

The Development Intelligence Engine answers five questions for any evaluated athlete:
1. **Where are you now?** - Current KR, component breakdown, strengths and gaps
2. **Where should you be?** - Best-fit competitive level based on KR and legend reads
3. **What are you worth there?** - Scholarship value at target programs
4. **What's the gap?** - Specific performance/technical targets for KR improvement
5. **What's the path?** - Development roadmap with projected timelines

## 2. PR PROJECTION CURVES

### 2.1 Age-Based PR Progression Models

Track and field athletes follow predictable PR progression curves by event group and age. These curves inform development projections.

**Sprints (100m, 200m, 400m):**
- Peak age range: 24-28 years old
- Rapid improvement phase: 16-22 (especially 18-21)
- Plateau phase: 22-28
- Decline begins: 29-30
- Annual improvement rate during rapid phase: 1-3% per year
- Annual improvement rate during plateau: 0-0.5% per year
- College years (18-22) represent the steepest part of the improvement curve

**Hurdles (110mH, 400mH):**
- Peak age range: 24-29 (slightly later due to technical development)
- Rapid improvement phase: 18-24
- Technical improvement can continue through mid-20s even as physical tools plateau
- Annual improvement rate during rapid phase: 1-3% per year

**Middle Distance (800m, 1500m):**
- Peak age range: 25-30
- Rapid improvement phase: 18-24
- Endurance base continues developing through mid-20s
- Annual improvement rate during rapid phase: 1-2.5% per year
- Cross country background athletes may have steeper college improvement curves

**Distance (3000mSC, 5000m, 10000m):**
- Peak age range: 27-33 (latest peak of any event group)
- Rapid improvement phase: 20-27
- Mileage base takes years to build, enabling later peaks
- Annual improvement rate during rapid phase: 1-3% per year
- East African athletes often peak earlier (more mileage from younger age)

**Jumps (HJ, LJ, TJ, PV):**
- Peak age range: 23-28 (PV may peak later due to technical development)
- Rapid improvement phase: 17-23
- Technical development can extend competitive peak
- Annual improvement rate during rapid phase: 1-3% per year
- PV improvement curve is the most technical-dependent

**Throws (SP, DT, HT, JT):**
- Peak age range: 26-32 (latest physical peak alongside distance)
- Rapid improvement phase: 20-26
- Strength development continues through mid-to-late 20s
- Annual improvement rate during rapid phase: 2-5% per year (throws show the largest improvement range)
- Technique refinement adds distance even as physical gains slow

**Decathlon:**
- Peak age range: 25-29
- Improvement curve is flatter because some events improve while others plateau
- Total points generally increase as technical events mature
- Speed events may decline slightly as strength/technique events gain

### 2.2 Collegiate PR Projection

For current college athletes, project end-of-career PR:

**Method:**
1. Current PR and age
2. Years of eligibility remaining
3. Apply event-group improvement curve for remaining years
4. Adjust for training quality (facility tier, coaching quality)
5. Adjust for injury history (injuries compress the improvement window)
6. Output: projected PR at end of college career with confidence range

**Training Quality Multiplier:**
| Facility/Coaching Tier | Multiplier |
|------------------------|-----------|
| Elite (top D1 program with elite facilities) | 1.10x improvement rate |
| Strong (good D1/D2 program with solid facilities) | 1.00x (baseline) |
| Average (mid-level program, adequate facilities) | 0.90x |
| Below Average (limited facilities, limited coaching expertise in event) | 0.80x |

### 2.3 PR Improvement Targets

For each athlete, identify the specific performance targets needed to reach next KR tier:

**Example:**
- Current: 100m PR 10.45 -> KR 89 (Conference Scorer at D1)
- Next tier: 10.30-10.45 -> KR 92-94 (NCAA Qualifier, Conference Champion)
- Target: improve to 10.30 or faster
- Time needed: at 2% annual improvement rate, 10.45 -> 10.24 in 1 year is aggressive but possible; 10.35 is a realistic 1-year target

## 3. DEVELOPMENT ROADMAP

### 3.1 Component KR Gap Analysis

For each component KR, identify the gap between current score and the target needed for next KR tier:

| Component | Current | Target | Gap | Priority |
|-----------|---------|--------|-----|----------|
| PKR | 89 | 92 | +3 | HIGH - need PR improvement |
| TKR | 85 | 88 | +3 | MEDIUM - technical refinement |
| AKR | 90 | 91 | +1 | LOW - already strong |
| CKR | 86 | 90 | +4 | HIGH - needs big-meet performance |
| IQKR | 88 | 89 | +1 | LOW - adequate |

Priority ranking: address the component KRs with the largest gaps that are most improvable within the development timeline.

### 3.2 Training Periodization Framework

Track and field training follows a periodization model aligned to the competitive calendar:

**Macrocycle (Full Year):**
- General Preparation Phase (Summer/Early Fall): 8-12 weeks
  - Build aerobic base, general strength, technical foundation
  - High volume, lower intensity
- Specific Preparation Phase (Late Fall/Early Winter): 6-8 weeks
  - Event-specific training increases
  - Speed development, technique refinement
  - Indoor competition preparation
- Competition Phase 1 - Indoor (Winter): 8-10 weeks
  - Reduced training volume, increased intensity
  - Indoor meets as development and qualification opportunities
  - Conference and national championship preparation
- Specific Preparation Phase 2 (Early Spring): 4-6 weeks
  - Transition from indoor to outdoor
  - Event-specific volume increases
  - Technical adjustments for outdoor conditions
- Competition Phase 2 - Outdoor (Spring): 10-12 weeks
  - Peak competition phase
  - Volume decreases, intensity at maximum
  - Conference, regional, national championship taper and peak

**Development Focus by Phase:**

| Phase | PKR Development | TKR Development | AKR/EKR Development | CKR Development | IQKR Development |
|-------|----------------|----------------|---------------------|----------------|-----------------|
| General Prep | Volume base | Drill work, video analysis | Strength, conditioning | N/A (no competition) | Film study, race planning |
| Specific Prep | Event-specific workouts | Refined technique under fatigue | Event-specific power/endurance | Low-key meets for practice | Practice race execution |
| Indoor Competition | Indoor PRs | Competition execution | Maintain, reduce injury risk | Indoor championships | Tactical execution in meets |
| Outdoor Specific Prep | Transition workouts | Outdoor-specific adjustments | Peak preparation | Build toward outdoor season | Adjust tactics for outdoor |
| Outdoor Competition | Outdoor PRs, peak | Execute technique under pressure | Maintain, taper | Championship meets | Championship race execution |

### 3.3 Event-Specific Development Priorities

**Sprints:**
- PKR: speed development (acceleration, max velocity, speed endurance work)
- TKR: start mechanics, drive phase, running form
- AKR: strength-to-weight ratio, plyometrics, reactive strength

**Hurdles:**
- PKR: hurdle-specific speed work
- TKR: hurdle drills (lead leg, trail leg, rhythm), walkovers, short approach hurdles
- AKR: flat sprint speed development, hip flexibility

**Middle Distance:**
- PKR: race-pace and faster-than-race-pace interval work
- EKR: aerobic base building, tempo runs, threshold work
- TKR: tactical practice in training (simulated race scenarios)

**Distance:**
- PKR: long intervals, tempo, race-specific workouts
- EKR: mileage progression, long runs, aerobic development
- TKR: tactical practice, group workouts simulating pack racing

**Jumps:**
- PKR: approach speed development, jumping-specific plyometrics
- TKR: technical drills (approach marks, takeoff mechanics, flight)
- AKR: sprint training, strength training, reactive strength

**Throws:**
- PKR: full-throw practice with competitive implements
- TKR: standing throws, half-turns, full technique drills, video analysis
- AKR: maximal strength development (squat, bench, clean), rotational power

**Decathlon:**
- Balance development across all 10 events
- Prioritize weakest events for training time (most points to gain)
- Manage training volume (can not specialize in all events simultaneously)

## 4. TRANSFER AND PLACEMENT INTELLIGENCE

### 4.1 Level Fit Assessment

Using the Level Tier Map (KR read against multiple level legends):
- Identify the levels where the athlete would be a scorer/champion/All-American
- Identify the levels where the athlete would be depth/non-scoring
- Recommend optimal competitive level for maximum development and impact

### 4.2 Program Fit Factors

Beyond competitive level, consider:
- **Event coaching expertise:** Does the program have a coach who specializes in the athlete's event?
- **Training group quality:** Are there training partners at or above the athlete's level?
- **Facility quality:** Does the program have the necessary facilities (weight room, throwing areas, pole vault pits, etc.)?
- **Academic fit:** Does the program meet the athlete's academic goals?
- **Geographic fit:** Climate, proximity to home, altitude considerations
- **Scholarship availability:** What scholarship level can the program offer for this event group?

### 4.3 Transfer Portal Value Assessment

For athletes entering the transfer portal:
1. Current KR and projected KR at new program
2. Event group gap analysis at target programs (does this athlete fill a scoring need?)
3. Scholarship value: what should the target program offer?
4. Eligibility remaining
5. Development upside at new program vs current program

## 5. Professional Track and Field Landscape

### 5.1 Professional Levels

**Tier 1: Global Elite**
- World Athletics Diamond League circuit
- World Championships / Olympics
- Athletes earning $100K-$1M+ annually from prize money and sponsorships
- Typically KR 95+ (using Pro Legend)

**Tier 2: Professional Circuit**
- Continental Tour (Gold, Silver, Bronze levels)
- National championships
- Athletes earning $30K-$100K annually
- Shoe company contracts (Nike, adidas, New Balance, Puma, ASICS, Hoka, On)
- KR 90-95 range

**Tier 3: Semi-Professional**
- Smaller professional meets
- National-level competitions
- Athletes earning $5K-$30K from athletics, may need supplementary income
- KR 85-90 range

**Tier 4: Post-Collegiate Competitive**
- Unattached athletes continuing to compete
- Club-level competition
- Minimal prize money
- KR 80-85 range

### 5.2 Professional Revenue Streams

**Prize Money:**
- Diamond League meeting: winner $10,000, scaled down through top 8
- Diamond League Final: winner $30,000
- World Championships: Gold $70,000, Silver $35,000, Bronze $22,000
- Olympics: varies by national federation
- Continental Tour: $3,000-$8,000 for winners
- US Championships: varies

**Shoe Company Contracts:**
- Tier 1 athletes: $100K-$500K+ annually (Nike, adidas headliners)
- Tier 2 athletes: $30K-$100K annually
- Tier 3 athletes: $10K-$30K or product-only deals
- Contract triggers: World/Olympic medals, top rankings, national titles
- Performance bonuses for medals and records

**Appearance Fees:**
- Top athletes receive fees to appear at meets
- Range: $2,000-$50,000+ depending on athlete stature and event
- Diamond League and major meet promoters pay appearance fees to attract stars

**Endorsements (beyond shoe companies):**
- Watch brands, nutrition companies, equipment
- Typically only for Tier 1 athletes with name recognition

### 5.3 Professional Development Path

**NCAA to Pro Timeline:**
1. Achieve NCAA All-American status or equivalent marks (KR 92+ at D1)
2. Compete at US Championships / national qualifying events
3. Attract shoe company attention (results-based)
4. Sign professional contract (typically after final college season or after using all eligibility)
5. Join professional training group
6. Target Diamond League entry and World Championship/Olympic qualification

**Key decision point:** Some athletes turn professional after junior year if they have elite marks. Others use 4-5 years of NCAA eligibility to develop. The decision should be based on:
- Current marks vs professional viability thresholds
- Development trajectory (still improving rapidly = stay in college)
- Shoe company interest (contract offers on the table)
- Olympic/World Championship qualification timeline

### 5.4 Olympic Trials Projection

For US athletes targeting the Olympics:
1. US Olympic Trials qualifying standard (set by USATF)
2. World Athletics qualifying standard (set by WA)
3. World Athletics ranking (backup qualifying pathway)
4. Project athlete's marks against these standards
5. Assess probability of qualifying (compare projected marks to standards)

**Qualifying pathways:**
- Entry standard: achieve the published mark by the qualifying deadline
- World Athletics ranking: accumulate points from competition results; top-ranked athletes qualify
- Relay pool: selected by national federation for relay teams
- Universality places: not applicable for US athletes (for smaller nations)

## 6. PRO TRANSITION KR MAPPING

### 6.1 College KR to Pro KR Translation

An athlete's college KR translates to the professional landscape via the Pro Legend (Pro_MTF_KR_Legend_v1):
- Same universal KR number
- Read against the Pro Legend for professional-level interpretation
- A KR of 92 at NCAA D1 is the same 92 at the pro level - it just reads differently on the Pro Legend (e.g., "competitive at Continental Tour level, developing Diamond League athlete")

### 6.2 Entry KR vs Peak KR

**Entry KR:** The athlete's KR at the time of turning professional (typically end of college career).

**Peak KR:** The projected maximum KR the athlete can achieve with professional-level training, coaching, and competition over a 3-5 year development window.

**Peak KR Projection:**
- Base: Entry KR
- Add: projected improvement from age-based PR curve (Section 2.1)
- Add: training quality improvement (professional coaching and facilities)
- Add: competition quality improvement (racing against stronger fields improves CKR)
- Cap: Entry KR + 8 maximum (even in best case, a 5-year pro development window is unlikely to produce more than 8 KR points of improvement)

## 7. COACHING IMPACT MODIFIER

### 7.1 Purpose

The Coaching Impact Modifier assesses how a specific coaching environment affects athlete development. It does NOT modify KR. It informs development projections and program fit recommendations.

### 7.2 Coaching Quality Indicators

| Indicator | High Impact | Moderate Impact | Low Impact |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|------------|
| Event-group specialization | Coach is a recognized specialist in the event | Coach has some experience in the event | Coach covers the event as part of broader duties |
| Athlete development track record | Multiple athletes improved 5%+ under this coach | Some athletes improved notably | No clear development pattern |
| NCAA qualifier production | Regularly produces NCAA qualifiers in the event | Occasional qualifiers | Rare or no qualifiers |
| Professional athlete production | Has coached professional-level athletes | Has coached near-professional athletes | No pro athletes |
| Facility quality | Elite event-specific facilities | Adequate facilities | Below-standard facilities |
| Training group quality | Multiple high-KR athletes in training group | Some quality training partners | Athlete trains largely alone |

### 7.3 Application

Coaching Impact Modifier adjusts the Development Roadmap timeline:
- High-impact coaching: development targets achieved 10-20% faster
- Moderate-impact coaching: baseline timeline
- Low-impact coaching: development targets take 10-20% longer or may not be achievable

## 8. REDSHIRT AND ELIGIBILITY MANAGEMENT

### 8.1 Redshirt Value Assessment

For incoming freshmen or transfer athletes:
- **Redshirt benefit:** Extra year of physical and technical development before using competitive eligibility
- **Redshirt cost:** One year of competitive experience and team point contribution

**Redshirt recommended when:**
- Athlete's current KR is more than 10 points below conference scoring threshold
- Athlete has clear physical development remaining (young for class, underdeveloped)
- Athlete is recovering from injury
- Athlete is transitioning to a new event (e.g., converting a sprinter to hurdles)

**Redshirt NOT recommended when:**
- Athlete can score conference points immediately
- Athlete is a transfer with limited eligibility remaining
- Athlete's event group has a team scoring gap that needs filling now
- Athlete is physically mature and improvement will come from competition experience

### 8.2 Fifth-Year / Sixth-Year Value

For athletes with remaining eligibility beyond 4 years (COVID eligibility, medical redshirt, academic redshirt):
- Project PR improvement with additional year
- Assess scholarship cost vs projected point contribution
- Compare against incoming recruit who could fill the same roster spot
