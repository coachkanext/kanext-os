# Institutional Network Strategy

**KaNeXT LLC | Confidential**

## Overview

The partner institution is the first institutional operating partner. South Florida Bible College & Theological Seminary (SFBC) is the Year 1 deployment, selected from among viable candidates for reasons documented in the Institution-Agnostic Deployment brief. SFBC is the beachhead. The campus built at Miami Lakes in Year 2 through Year 5 becomes the national flagship.

Around the partner institution and the flagship campus, KaNeXT builds a network of Christian, values-aligned institutions connected under Institutional Operating Agreements (IOAs). Each school retains its identity, accreditation, and community. Each operates on the same OS, under the same IOA structure, benefiting from the same shared infrastructure.

The sister and brother schools are regional nodes that extend the mission, expand the platform, and create institutional presence across the country.

## The Mission

Many families and students want Christian, mission-aligned institutions where governance, culture, and community reinforce their values. That demand is real and growing, but the supply of viable Christian institutions is shrinking. Providence Christian College, Trinity Christian College, Birmingham-Southern, Eastern Nazarene, Clarks Summit, Lincoln Christian University, and Johnson University Florida all closed in 2024-2025.

KaNeXT builds the operating infrastructure that allows these institutions to remain viable and grow. The network is not defined by what it opposes. It is defined by what it provides: faith-centered education, values-aligned community, and the operational sustainability that standalone small Christian colleges increasingly cannot achieve on their own.

This is not a denominational network. Schools from different Christian traditions, different regions, and different academic specializations join because they share a commitment to mission-aligned education and because KaNeXT provides the operational infrastructure to make that education sustainable.

## The Partner Institution: The First Deployment and Future Flagship

The partner institution is the Year 1 operating partner. SFBC is a 40-year-old evangelical Christian institution, ABHE-accredited in good standing (reaffirmation cycle 2028), Title IV-eligible, with approximately 1,100 enrollment and existing infrastructure (Populi SIS since 2012, 424 international students from 43 countries, MBA launched Fall 2025, BSN in development, GAAC charter conference membership).

Year 1 operates under an Athletics-Only Agreement (AOA). Year 2 expands to a Full IOA upon ABHE substantive change approval and DOE CIO determination. Under the Full IOA, KaNeXT operates institutional infrastructure (enrollment, platform deployment, athletic operations, campus operations as Miami Lakes comes online) while SFBC retains academic authority, curriculum governance, faculty, and ABHE accreditation.

The flagship state of the partner institution is a function of the Miami Lakes campus buildout over Year 2 through Year 5. Year 3 Phase 1 operational. Year 4 majority operations. Year 5 full operations at what the revenue model describes as the Christian HPU scale: 24,600-27,700 total enrollment, Division I athletic trajectory, tuition approaching $20,000-$23,500, and the national institutional brand the network is anchored around.

The flagship is not complete on Day 1. It is built.

## The Sister and Brother Schools

The network schools are not mini-flagships. They are their own institutions with their own strengths, their own programs, and their own communities. What they share is the operating system and the mission.

**A nursing school in the Midwest.** Small enrollment, accredited nursing program, Christian identity, campus with room to grow. KaNeXT deploys the OS, activates enrollment infrastructure, connects the school to the network's financial and communication systems. Students use KPay for tuition and campus life. Faculty use Dipson for institutional operations. The school may field small NAIA or USCAA athletic teams, or it may not field athletics at all. The value is the institutional node: enrollment, housing, OS deployment, and regional presence.

**A business school on the West Coast.** Accredited business programs, conservative values, struggling enrollment. KaNeXT deploys the IOA, provides capital for campus improvement, activates the enrollment company, and connects the school to the national network. Students can take courses at any network school. Faculty can collaborate across institutions. The business school produces graduates who enter the workforce already embedded in the KaNeXT ecosystem.

**A small Christian college in the Southeast.** Facing closure, 400 students, beautiful campus, deep community roots. KaNeXT acquires the campus through philanthropic transfer, deploys the IOA, rebuilds enrollment through the shared infrastructure. The school keeps its name, its accreditation, its local identity. It gains the OS, the financial infrastructure, the enrollment engine, and connection to a national network that gives it visibility and resources it could never achieve alone.

**A trade or vocational school.** Accredited programs in skilled trades, healthcare, technology. Christian values. Regional labor market need. KaNeXT provides the same infrastructure: OS, enrollment, financial systems, communication, housing. The school trains the workforce while the OS captures the institutional economics.

Each school is different. The network does not impose a single model. It provides the operating infrastructure that allows each school to do what it does best while benefiting from shared systems that no small institution could build on its own.

## Depth Over Breadth at the Institutional Layer

The partner institution is the only institutional operating partner during the current planning horizon. This is intentional. The thesis is depth, not breadth.

KaNeXT does not spread Year 1 capital and attention across multiple distressed institutional acquisitions. KaNeXT deploys the full architecture at one institution and produces publicly attributable transformation: athletic championships, D1 upsets, enrollment growth, tuition premium, campus development, retention improvement, and institutional financial transformation.

The FOMO pipeline is the market response to visible transformation. Other institutions reach out as the partner institution matures through the partnership. These inbound institutions become potential future deployment opportunities after the partner institution is fully built out, not concurrent deployments dividing Year 1 attention. The multiplication at the institutional operating partner layer happens after the partner institution reaches the transformation markers that justify a second institutional deployment.

Platform multiplication (every user, every church, every business, every family, every mandate institution) operates at the user and organizational level concurrently with Year 1. The institutional operating partner side stays disciplined on the partner institution.

## What Every Network School Gets

Every school in the network operates under an IOA and receives:

**KaNeXT OS.** The full institutional operating system: KPay for all financial transactions, KTV for media and communication, Dipson for institutional intelligence, VoIP for communication, and the full platform across all five modes.

**Enrollment infrastructure.** The enrollment operation activates enrollment growth for every network school. National and regional marketing, lead generation, application processing, and enrollment conversion powered by the same system that drives the partner institution's growth.

**Financial infrastructure.** KPay processes all institutional transactions. The bank (upon charter acquisition) provides campus banking. Wallet adoption among students, faculty, and families generates deposit volume, commerce processing, and financial data.

**Intelligence.** If the school fields athletic teams, the full intelligence architecture is available. If it does not, institutional intelligence (admissions, hiring, enrollment management, retention) still operates. Every school benefits from governed decision-making.

**Cluster housing.** KaNeXT acquires or leases residential property near each network school. Students and athletes create predictable housing demand. All transactions process through KPay. The housing serves both the school and the surrounding community, creating a real estate node at every network location.

**National brand association.** Connection to the partner institution, the Miami Lakes flagship as it develops, and the KaNeXT network provides visibility and credibility that a standalone small institution cannot generate. A prospective student choosing between an isolated 400-student college and the same college as part of a national Christian education network with the Miami Lakes flagship at its center makes a different decision.

## The Acquisition Environment

Christian institutions are closing at an accelerating rate. The common profile: small enrollment, tuition dependency, minimal endowment, governance instability, and no operating infrastructure capable of reversing decline. Few have access to a partner with the capital, technology, and institutional operating capability to transform their trajectory.

KaNeXT approaches boards facing closure with a specific offer: operational rescue through capital, technology, and the IOA model proven at the partner institution. The school keeps its accreditation, its name, its mission, and its community. KaNeXT provides everything else.

Terms reflect the distressed acquisition environment. A school facing closure has limited alternatives. Campus land costs for struggling institutions are expected to be well below replacement value. Each acquisition adds an accreditation, a campus, a student body, and a regional node to the network.

## Target Criteria

**Accreditation (critical).** Active regional or recognized national accreditation. Without it, there is no institution. Warning or probation acceptable if issues are operational.

**Christian or mission-aligned identity.** The network is built around shared values. Faith-based institutional identity is the primary alignment criterion.

**Academic programs with regional relevance.** Nursing, business, education, healthcare, technology, trades. The network does not need every school to offer every program. Specialization is an advantage.

**Campus or access to land.** Physical campus with expansion capacity. Cluster housing potential in the surrounding area.

**Small enrollment.** Under 2,500 students. Enrollment trajectory matters more than current level. The IOA model is designed to reverse decline.

**Financial distress.** Creates the acquisition opportunity and shapes terms.

**Geography.** Regional distribution for national coverage. Each new school should expand the network's geographic footprint rather than duplicate existing coverage.

Athletics is not required. Schools with athletic programs can activate the multi-level system. Schools without athletics contribute to the network through enrollment, OS deployment, housing, and regional presence.

## Intelligence-Governed Acquisition

Every target school is evaluated through the acquisition intelligence system. Four dimensions: Financial KR (balance sheet health, revenue trajectory, cost structure), Operational KR (management quality, academic viability, infrastructure condition), Regulatory KR (accreditation risk, Title IV, state compliance), and System KR (geographic fit, enrollment potential, campus value, integration complexity).

The intelligence prevents bad acquisitions. A school that scores poorly on regulatory risk does not proceed regardless of how attractive the price or the narrative. The same discipline that governs player evaluation governs institutional evaluation.

## Network Scale

A network of 10-20 institutions distributed across key regions creates national coverage. Each school serves its region. Each contributes to the shared enrollment pipeline, shared financial infrastructure, and shared platform economics. The infrastructure (enrollment company, fulfillment center, intelligence system, KTV, KPay, the bank) is built once and serves every school. Marginal cost per school added decreases as the network grows.

The network is a federation, not a chain. Each school maintains its own identity and its own community. What makes it a network is the shared operating system, the shared mission, and the shared connection to the partner institution and Miami Lakes flagship as the national anchor.
