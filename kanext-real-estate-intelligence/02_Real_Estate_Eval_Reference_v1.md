## COMPONENT KRs

### LKR - Location KR (0-100)

Location KR measures the intrinsic locational value of the property independent of what is built on it. Location is the one attribute that cannot be changed after acquisition.

**Scoring dimensions:**

- **Proximity to institutional assets (25%):** Distance to KaNeXT-affiliated schools, mandate campuses, FMU, or other KaNeXT-owned properties. Properties within 1 mile of a mandate school score 90+. Properties within 3 miles score 75-89. Properties beyond 5 miles with no institutional proximity score below 60.

- **Transportation access (20%):** Highway frontage or interchange proximity, public transit access (bus, rail, BRT), airport proximity for campus properties. Properties with direct highway access and multiple transit options score 90+. Properties accessible only by local roads with no transit score below 60.

- **Neighborhood quality (20%):** Crime rates relative to county average, median household income, property value trends, visual condition of surrounding properties, code enforcement activity. Neighborhoods with below-average crime, rising incomes, and stable or improving property values score 80+.

- **Growth trajectory (15%):** Population growth rate, employment growth, new construction permits, business formation rates, rezoning activity. Areas with 2%+ annual population growth and active development pipeline score 85+. Declining population areas score below 50.

- **Walkability and amenity proximity (10%):** Walk Score equivalent, distance to grocery, pharmacy, restaurants, retail, parks, medical facilities. Campus properties require proximity to student-serving amenities. Cluster housing requires grocery and transit within walking distance.

- **Safety metrics (10%):** Crime index, sex offender proximity, emergency response times, flood/hurricane risk, wildfire risk. Properties in high-crime areas or FEMA flood zones without mitigation score below 60 regardless of other attributes.

---

### FKR - Financial KR (0-100)

Financial KR measures the economic value proposition of the property: what it costs, what it generates, and what it will be worth.

**Scoring dimensions:**

- **Acquisition cost vs market (25%):** Price per acre or per sqft compared to recent comparable sales in the submarket. Acquisition at or below market scores 80+. Acquisition at 10%+ premium requires justification in Strategic KR. Acquisition at 20%+ premium caps FKR at 70.

- **Projected appreciation (15%):** Based on submarket trends, development pipeline, and demographic drivers. Markets with 5%+ annual appreciation and strong fundamentals score 85+. Flat or declining markets score below 60.

- **Rental yield potential (15%):** For income-producing properties and cluster housing. Gross rental yield above 8% scores 85+. Yield between 5-8% scores 70-84. Yield below 5% scores under 70. For non-income properties (raw campus land), this dimension is weighted at 0% and redistributed to other dimensions.

- **Development cost estimates (15%):** Total cost to develop the property for its intended use per square foot of buildable area. Below-market construction costs (due to favorable topography, existing infrastructure, or scale) score 80+. Above-market construction costs (difficult terrain, infrastructure gaps, environmental remediation) score below 65.

- **Operating expense ratio (10%):** Projected operating expenses as a percentage of revenue for operating properties. OER below 35% scores 85+. OER 35-50% scores 70-84. OER above 50% scores below 70. For non-operating properties, this dimension is weighted at 0% and redistributed.

- **Cap rate (10%):** Net operating income divided by acquisition cost. For institutional campus properties, cap rate is benchmarked against HBCU and higher-ed comparable facilities rather than commercial real estate. Cap rate above 7% scores 85+. Cap rate 4-7% scores 70-84. Cap rate below 4% scores below 70.

- **Debt service coverage (10%):** For properties financed with HBCU Capital Financing or other debt. DSCR above 1.5x scores 85+. DSCR 1.2-1.5x scores 70-84. DSCR below 1.2x scores below 65 and triggers a risk flag.

---

### DKR - Development KR (0-100)

Development KR measures how ready the property is to become what KaNeXT needs it to be.

**Scoring dimensions:**

- **Zoning compatibility (25%):** Current zoning vs intended use. If current zoning permits the intended use by right, score 90+. If a variance or special exception is needed, score 70-89 depending on likelihood of approval. If full rezoning is required, score 50-69. If rezoning is politically infeasible, score below 40.

- **Environmental clearance (20%):** Phase I Environmental Site Assessment results. Clean Phase I with no recognized environmental conditions scores 90+. Phase I with minor conditions requiring Phase II investigation scores 70-89. Known contamination requiring remediation scores below 60. No environmental assessment conducted caps DKR at 65 and triggers a confidence penalty.

- **Utility availability (20%):** Water, sewer, electric, gas, and fiber/broadband. All utilities available at the property boundary with sufficient capacity scores 90+. Utilities available but requiring extension (less than 0.5 miles) scores 75-89. Utilities requiring significant extension (0.5+ miles) or capacity upgrades scores below 70.

- **Topography and buildability (15%):** Flat, well-drained land with stable soil scores 90+. Moderate grading required scores 75-89. Significant grade changes, drainage issues, wetlands mitigation, or poor soil conditions score below 70. Rocky substrate or high water table requiring specialized foundations score below 60.

- **Existing structures (10%):** Existing structures that are assets (usable buildings, historic structures eligible for tax credits, infrastructure that reduces development cost) score 80+. No existing structures (raw land) score 70 (neutral). Existing structures that are liabilities (requiring demolition, containing hazardous materials, or structurally unsound) score below 60.

- **Entitlement timeline (10%):** Estimated time from acquisition to construction permit. Under 6 months scores 90+. 6-12 months scores 75-89. 12-24 months scores 60-74. Over 24 months scores below 60.

---

### SKR - Strategic KR (0-100)

Strategic KR measures how well this property serves the KaNeXT institutional mission and long-term vision.

**Scoring dimensions:**

- **Campus master plan alignment (30%):** Does this property directly serve the 596-acre Miami Lakes campus vision or the FMU campus transition? Properties that are part of the primary campus footprint score 95+. Properties adjacent to the campus with expansion potential score 80-94. Properties that serve institutional needs but are geographically separate (cluster housing near mandate schools) score 65-79 based on institutional demand. Properties with no clear connection to the master plan score below 60.

- **Proximity to other KaNeXT properties (15%):** Contiguous or adjacent parcels to existing holdings score 90+. Properties within 1 mile of existing holdings score 75-89. Properties in the same submarket score 60-74. Isolated properties score below 60.

- **Student/athlete housing demand (20%):** Based on enrollment projections and athletic roster sizes at nearby mandate schools. Areas with 200+ students/athletes needing housing score 85+. Areas with 50-199 needing housing score 70-84. Areas with under 50 score below 70. For non-housing properties, this dimension is redistributed.

- **Institutional visibility and brand value (15%):** Properties with highway frontage, prominent street presence, or landmark potential score 85+. Properties with standard street presence score 70-84. Properties hidden, in industrial zones, or in locations that detract from institutional image score below 65.

- **Expansion potential (20%):** Adjacent parcels available for future acquisition. Three or more adjacent parcels available score 85+. One to two adjacent parcels score 70-84. Landlocked with no expansion possibility scores below 60.

## KLVN NORMALIZATION

### Lambda by Market Tier

Market tier captures the baseline cost, regulation, and development complexity of different geographic markets. All properties are evaluated within their market context.

| Market Tier | Lambda | Description | Examples |
|------------|--------|-------------|----------|
| Tier 1 - Primary | 1.000 | Major metro, high cost, high regulation, high demand. Highest complexity and price point. | Miami-Dade County, FL |
| Tier 2 - Secondary Metro | 0.925 | Mid-size metro, moderate cost and regulation, strong demand. | Broward County, FL; Palm Beach County, FL; Orlando metro; Atlanta metro |
| Tier 3 - Tertiary Metro | 0.850 | Smaller metro, lower cost, moderate regulation. | Jacksonville, FL; Tampa suburbs; Charlotte metro; Nashville metro |
| Tier 4 - Small City/Exurban | 0.775 | Small city or exurban, low cost, lighter regulation, variable demand. | College towns, rural-adjacent suburbs, small Southern cities |
| Tier 5 - Rural | 0.700 | Rural, lowest cost, minimal regulation, limited demand. Property values are lower but so is the competitive landscape. | Rural campus sites, agricultural land near small institutions |

**Application:** When comparing properties across market tiers, normalize the Financial KR by lambda. A property at KR 85 in Tier 1 (Miami-Dade) is a stronger financial position than KR 85 in Tier 4 because the Tier 1 property was scored against a higher-cost, higher-competition baseline.

## MARKET DATA SOURCES

### Primary Sources (Tier 1 - Official Records)
- **County Property Appraiser:** Assessed values, tax history, ownership, parcel boundaries, zoning, legal description. Miami-Dade Property Appraiser is primary for South Florida.
- **Municipal Zoning Department:** Current zoning maps, future land use maps, pending applications, hearing schedules.
- **Clerk of Court:** Title records, liens, mortgages, judgments, lis pendens.
- **Environmental Protection Agency / State DEP:** Environmental records, brownfield registry, superfund sites, underground storage tanks.
- **FEMA:** Flood maps, flood zone designations, community rating system status.

### Secondary Sources (Tier 2 - Market Data)
- **MLS / Commercial Listing Services:** Recent comparable sales, active listings, days on market, price per sqft trends.
- **CoStar / LoopNet:** Commercial property data, vacancy rates, rental rates, cap rates, market reports.
- **US Census Bureau:** Population, household income, demographic composition, housing characteristics.
- **Bureau of Labor Statistics:** Employment data, industry composition, wage trends.
- **County Building Department:** Permits issued, construction starts, certificate of occupancy records.

### Tertiary Sources (Tier 3 - Contextual)
- **NCES College Navigator / IPEDS:** Enrollment data for nearby institutions (for cluster housing demand modeling).
- **Local news sources:** Development pipeline, political context, community opposition, infrastructure projects.
- **Google Maps / Satellite Imagery:** Visual inspection, surrounding land use, access roads, topography.
- **Walk Score:** Walkability, transit score, bike score.
- **NOAA / National Weather Service:** Climate data, hurricane history, flood history.

## DEAL STRUCTURE TEMPLATES

### Template 1: Cash Acquisition (Outright Purchase)
**Use when:** Standard market transaction. Seller wants clean close. Property is available on open market.
- Purchase price paid in full at closing.
- Title insurance and survey required.
- Environmental Phase I required; Phase II if Phase I identifies conditions.
- Standard 30-60 day due diligence period.
- Closing costs estimated at 2-3% of purchase price.
- **Current KaNeXT target using this structure:** 596 acres Miami Lakes ($330M).

### Template 2: Philanthropic Transfer (Deed Gift)
**Use when:** Property is transferred as a charitable contribution, typically from a nonprofit or institution. KaNeXT (through the fund) provides philanthropic capital to the institution in exchange for the deed.
- Not a traditional sale. Structured as a philanthropic gift.
- Tax-deductible for the institution/donor.
- Property appraisal required for gift valuation.
- Leaseback arrangement during transition period (institution continues to operate on the property).
- Title search and environmental assessment still required.
- **Current KaNeXT target using this structure:** 52.6 acres FMU campus, Miami Gardens ($100M philanthropic capital in exchange for deed transfer).

### Template 3: Leaseback Arrangement
**Use when:** Property is acquired but the seller or occupant continues to use the property for a defined period.
- Provider acquires title.
- Seller/occupant leases back at nominal rate ($1/year in the FMU model).
- Lease period defined by operational transition timeline.
- Maintenance and operating cost responsibility defined in lease.
- **Integrated with:** IOA Phase 1 through Phase 3 transition timeline. FMU operates on existing campus until new campus facilities are ready.

### Template 4: Portfolio Acquisition (Multiple Properties)
**Use when:** Acquiring a cluster of residential properties for student housing near a mandate school.
- Bundle multiple properties into a single transaction where possible.
- Negotiate portfolio discount (typically 5-15% below individual market value for bulk purchase).
- Phase acquisitions if capital constraints require it (acquire highest-KR properties first).
- Property management structure established before closing (in-house or third-party).
- **Use case:** Cluster housing near mandate schools.

### Template 5: Land Bank / Option Agreement
**Use when:** Property is strategically important but not immediately needed. Secure the right to acquire without deploying full capital now.
- Option fee (typically 1-5% of purchase price) secures right to purchase within defined period.
- Purchase price locked at option execution.
- Option period aligned with IOA phase triggers (e.g., option exercised when Phase 3 campus construction begins).
- Reduces capital deployed until the property is actually needed.
- **Use case:** Adjacent parcels to Miami Lakes campus for phased expansion.

### Template 6: Development Joint Venture
**Use when:** Property requires development that exceeds KaNeXT's construction capacity or expertise.
- KaNeXT contributes land or capital. Development partner contributes construction expertise and execution.
- Ownership split defined at outset (KaNeXT typically retains majority ownership and operational control).
- Development partner compensated through development fees and/or minority equity.
- KaNeXT retains right to manage completed property through KaNeXT OS.
- **Use case:** Large-scale campus construction where third-party general contractor operates under JV structure rather than fee-for-service.

## INTEGRATION RISK FLAGS

These are conditions that, if present, elevate the risk of integrating a property into the KaNeXT ecosystem. Any HIGH or CRITICAL flag requires explicit discussion and mitigation plan before proceeding.

| Flag | Severity Levels | Description |
|------|----------------|-------------|
| **Zoning Incompatibility** | LOW: Variance needed (likely). MEDIUM: Rezoning needed (uncertain). HIGH: Rezoning needed (politically opposed). CRITICAL: Intended use prohibited and no legal pathway. | Current zoning does not permit the intended use. |
| **Environmental Contamination** | LOW: Phase I clean. MEDIUM: Phase I conditions, Phase II needed. HIGH: Known contamination, remediation plan exists. CRITICAL: Known contamination, no remediation plan, or Superfund-listed. | Contamination present or suspected on the property. |
| **Title Defect** | LOW: Minor encumbrances (utility easements). MEDIUM: Material encumbrances requiring negotiation. HIGH: Liens or judgments that must be cleared before close. CRITICAL: Disputed ownership, ongoing litigation, or clouded title. | Title is not clean. |
| **Flood/Natural Hazard** | LOW: Outside all flood zones, low hurricane exposure. MEDIUM: In 500-year flood zone with mitigation available. HIGH: In 100-year flood zone. CRITICAL: In floodway or coastal high-hazard area. | Property is exposed to natural hazard risk. |
| **Infrastructure Gap** | LOW: All utilities at boundary. MEDIUM: Utility extension under 0.5 miles. HIGH: Utility extension over 1 mile or capacity upgrade needed. CRITICAL: No utility access feasible without major public investment. | Property lacks necessary infrastructure. |
| **Access Limitation** | LOW: Multiple access points from public roads. MEDIUM: Single access point. HIGH: Access via private road or easement. CRITICAL: Landlocked with no legal access. | Property cannot be easily reached. |
| **Community Opposition** | LOW: No known opposition. MEDIUM: Informal concern from neighbors. HIGH: Organized opposition group. CRITICAL: Active litigation or injunction blocking development. | Local community opposes the intended use. |
| **Construction Cost Escalation** | LOW: Stable material and labor costs in the submarket. MEDIUM: 5-10% above national average. HIGH: 10-20% above national average or contractor scarcity. CRITICAL: 20%+ above national average or no qualified contractors available. | Development cost exceeds budget assumptions. |
